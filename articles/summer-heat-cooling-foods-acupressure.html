Summer Heat Relief: 6 TCM Cooling Soups and 4 Acupressure Points to Beat the Heat

📅 June 25, 2026 • Nutrition
Advertisement Space (728x90)

As summer temperatures climb, many people reach for ice-cold drinks, air conditioning, and frozen treats to find relief. But according to Traditional Chinese Medicine (TCM), these quick fixes can actually make things worse — weakening your digestive system and creating a cycle of internal heat that's harder to break. Instead, TCM offers a more intelligent approach: cooling the body from the inside out with the right foods, teas, and acupressure techniques that have been refined over thousands of years.

In this guide, you'll discover six nourishing Chinese cooling soups that quench heat without shocking your system, four powerful acupressure points that provide instant relief, and lifestyle wisdom to help you stay energized and comfortable throughout the hottest months. These are not just recipes — they are a philosophy of living in harmony with the seasons.

Key Takeaways

  • TCM views summer heat as an imbalance between external heat and the body's internal cooling system (Yin)
  • Cooling foods like mung beans, watermelon rind, and chrysanthemum tea naturally lower body temperature
  • Four acupressure points can provide instant relief from heat-related discomfort
  • Avoiding excessive cold drinks prevents the spleen and stomach from weakening
  • Aligning your lifestyle with summer's natural rhythm prevents heat exhaustion and fatigue

Understanding Summer Heat in TCM

In Western medicine, summer heat is simply a rise in ambient temperature that the body must regulate through sweating and vasodilation. TCM takes a more nuanced view, recognizing that the body's relationship with external heat is deeply connected to its internal balance of Yin and Yang.

The Liver and Gallbladder are particularly affected by summer heat. When external heat combines with internal fire — from stress, spicy foods, alcohol, or lack of sleep — the result is what TCM calls "Summer Heat" (暑, Shu). This isn't just feeling warm; it's a pattern of symptoms that includes fatigue, irritability, heavy sweating, thirst, poor appetite, and even digestive upset.

The key principle TCM teaches is this: when it's hot outside, cool from within. This doesn't mean consuming ice — it means nourishing the body's Yin (cooling, moistening) energy so it can naturally counterbalance the external heat.

"In summer, eat cool to clear heat, drink warm to protect the spleen, and rest in shade to preserve Yin."

— Traditional Chinese Medicine proverb

6 Nourishing Chinese Cooling Soups

In Chinese households, summer is the season of soups. These are not heavy, warming winter stews — they are light, clear, and designed to hydrate, cool, and nourish the body simultaneously. Each recipe uses ingredients that TCM classifies as "cool" or "cold" in nature, meaning they naturally lower body temperature and clear heat without the side effects of ice-cold beverages.

1. Mung Bean Soup (绿豆汤) — The Classic Summer Cooler

The most iconic summer drink in Chinese culture. Mung beans are considered "cool" in nature and are renowned for their ability to clear heat, detoxify, and reduce inflammation. This simple soup is a staple in Chinese homes during summer, often served chilled but not ice-cold.

Recipe: Rinse 1 cup of mung beans. Add to a pot with 8 cups of water and 2 slices of ginger (optional, to protect the spleen). Bring to a boil, then simmer for 30–40 minutes until the beans split open. Add rock sugar to taste. Serve warm or at room temperature. For a clearer soup, simmer gently; for a thicker version, cook longer.

Benefits: Clears summer heat, detoxifies, reduces inflammation, hydrates the body, soothes irritable skin.

2. Watermelon Rind Soup (西瓜皮汤) — Zero-Waste Cooling

Don't throw away the white part of the watermelon! In TCM, watermelon rind (西瓜翠衣) is considered even more cooling than the flesh. It's one of the most effective natural remedies for summer heat, and this soup turns food waste into medicine.

Recipe: Peel the green skin from watermelon rind, leaving the white part. Cut into cubes. Add to a pot with 6 cups of water, a handful of mung beans, and 3 red dates. Simmer for 30 minutes. Season lightly with salt. Serve warm.

Benefits: Clears heat, promotes urination, reduces water retention, nourishes the stomach without being too cold for the spleen.

3. Chrysanthemum and Goji Berry Tea (菊花枸杞茶) — The Eye-Soothing Cooler

A beloved summer beverage in Chinese teahouses. Chrysanthemum flowers are one of TCM's most important cooling herbs, specifically targeting heat in the head and eyes. Combined with goji berries that nourish the liver, this tea is both refreshing and therapeutic.

Recipe: Add 10–15 dried chrysanthemum flowers and 1 tablespoon of goji berries to a teapot. Pour boiling water over them and steep for 5–10 minutes. Add a small amount of rock sugar or honey if desired. Drink 1–2 times daily.

Benefits: Clears heat from the eyes, soothes headaches, calms irritability, nourishes the liver, provides gentle hydration.

4. Winter Melon and Rib Soup (冬瓜排骨汤) — The Hydrating Classic

A quintessential summer soup in southern China. Winter melon is extremely cooling in nature and is one of the best foods for clearing summer heat and promoting urination. Combined with pork ribs, it becomes a complete, nourishing meal that hydrates without heaviness.

Recipe: Blanch 500g pork ribs in boiling water for 2 minutes, then rinse. Peel and cube 500g winter melon. Add ribs, winter melon, 6 cups of water, 3 slices of ginger, and 2 red dates to a pot. Bring to a boil, then simmer for 40 minutes. Season with salt and a pinch of white pepper.

Benefits: Clears heat, promotes urination, reduces water retention, provides protein and calcium, extremely hydrating.

5. Lotus Seed and Lily Bulb Soup (莲子百合糖水) — The Calming Sweet Soup

A sweet dessert soup that cools both body and mind. Lotus seeds calm the heart and strengthen the spleen, while lily bulbs moisten the lungs and clear heat. This traditional Cantonese sweet soup (糖水) is perfect for summer evenings when heat makes it hard to sleep.

Recipe: Soak 1 cup lotus seeds and ½ cup dried lily bulbs in water for 2 hours. Add to a pot with 6 cups of water and 2 tablespoons of rock sugar. Simmer for 40 minutes until lotus seeds are soft. Serve warm or chilled. Add a handful of goji berries in the last 5 minutes.

Benefits: Calms the mind, promotes sleep, moistens the lungs, clears heart fire, nourishes the spleen.

6. Green Bean and Seaweed Soup (绿豆海带汤) — The Detox Duo

A powerful combination for deep summer detoxification. Both mung beans and seaweed are cooling in nature, making this soup a potent remedy for heat rashes, skin breakouts, and the heavy, sluggish feeling that summer heat can bring.

Recipe: Rinse 1 cup mung beans and soak 2 pieces of dried seaweed (kelp) for 30 minutes. Add both to a pot with 8 cups of water. Bring to a boil, then simmer for 40 minutes. Add a small piece of pork bone or chicken for extra nourishment if desired. Season with salt.

Benefits: Clears heat, detoxifies, reduces inflammation, soothes skin rashes, promotes urination, supports thyroid health.

4 Acupressure Points for Summer Heat Relief

When heat makes you feel dizzy, headachy, or exhausted, acupressure can provide immediate relief. These four points are specifically chosen for their ability to clear summer heat, cool the body, and restore energy.

1. Quchi (LI11) — Pool at the Crook

Location: At the outer end of the elbow crease, in the depression at the center of the lateral epicondyle of the humerus.

LI11 is the single most important acupressure point for clearing heat from the entire body. It's part of the Large Intestine meridian and has a powerful cooling effect. When you feel overheated, fatigued, or have a summer headache, press this point firmly for 2–3 minutes on each arm. You can also run cool water over this area for added effect.

2. Neiguan (PC6) — Inner Pass

Location: Three finger-widths from the wrist crease, between the two tendons on the inner forearm.

PC6 is famous for relieving nausea and heat-related dizziness. It calms the heart, clears heat, and restores mental clarity. This point is especially useful when summer heat causes a feeling of stuffiness in the chest, nausea, or a foggy mind. Press gently but firmly for 2–3 minutes on each wrist.

3. Yanglingquan (GB34) — Yang Mound Spring

Location: In the depression in front of and below the head of the fibula (outer lower leg).

GB34 is the meeting point of all sinews and tendons in the body. It's excellent for summer heat that manifests as muscle cramps, leg heaviness, or joint stiffness. This point also clears heat from the Gallbladder meridian, which is particularly active during summer. Press for 2–3 minutes on each leg.

4. Yinlingquan (SP9) — Yin Mound Spring

Location: On the inner lower leg, in the depression below the inner knee where the bone curves inward.

SP9 is the master point for clearing dampness from the body — a common problem in hot, humid summer weather. When heat combines with dampness, you feel heavy, sluggish, and bloated. SP9 resolves this by promoting urination and clearing heat from the Spleen meridian. Press for 2–3 minutes on each leg.

How to Use Acupressure for Heat Relief

  • Timing: Press these points whenever you feel overheated, fatigued, or headachy during summer
  • Technique: Use your thumb to apply firm, steady pressure in small circular motions
  • Duration: Hold each point for 2–3 minutes, breathing deeply
  • Combination: Pair with sipping warm chrysanthemum tea for maximum cooling effect
  • Cooling hack: For LI11, run cool (not ice-cold) water over the point while pressing for enhanced relief

Foods to Eat in Summer According to TCM

TCM classifies foods by their thermal nature — warming, neutral, or cooling. In summer, emphasizing cooling foods helps the body maintain balance without relying on artificial cooling.

Cooling Fruits

Watermelon, pear, lychee (in moderation), cucumber, and mung bean sprouts are all excellent summer choices. They hydrate, cool, and provide essential vitamins. Eat them at room temperature for best results.

Cooling Vegetables

Bitter melon, winter melon, celery, tomato, and loofah (luffa) are among the most cooling vegetables. Bitter melon in particular is prized in TCM for its ability to clear heat and brighten the eyes.

Cooling Teas and Herbs

Chrysanthemum tea, honeysuckle tea (金银花茶), and lotus leaf tea (荷叶茶) are traditional summer beverages that clear heat gently and effectively. Sip them throughout the day instead of reaching for cold drinks.

Cooling Grains

Barley (薏米) and mung beans are considered cooling grains in TCM. Barley water is a classic summer drink that clears dampness and heat, while mung bean porridge provides sustained energy without heating the body.

Foods to Limit in Summer

Excessive Cold and Ice

Iced drinks, ice cream, and frozen foods shock the spleen and stomach, weakening their ability to digest and absorb nutrients. While they provide momentary relief, they actually impair the body's natural cooling mechanism and can lead to more heat accumulation internally.

Greasy and Fried Foods

Heavy, oily foods create internal dampness and heat, making you feel sluggish and bloated. Summer is the time for light, clear cooking methods — steaming, boiling, and quick stir-frying.

Spicy Foods (in excess)

While a small amount of spice can help the body release heat through sweat, excessive spicy food adds fire to an already hot system. Reduce chili, hot pepper, and heavily spiced dishes during the hottest months.

Late-Night Eating

Eating late at night forces the digestive system to work when it should be resting, generating internal heat. TCM recommends finishing dinner by 7 PM in summer to allow the body to cool down naturally before sleep.

Summer Lifestyle Tips from TCM

Beyond food and acupressure, TCM offers practical lifestyle wisdom for thriving in summer heat.

Rise Early, Rest at Midday

Summer is the season of Yang energy, which peaks at midday. TCM recommends waking early (around 5–6 AM) to enjoy the gentle morning energy, and taking a short rest or nap during the hottest hours (11 AM–2 PM). This simple habit prevents heat exhaustion and preserves energy for the afternoon.

Protect Your Head from Direct Sun

In TCM, the head is where all Yang meridians converge. Direct midday sun can cause heat to accumulate in the head, leading to headaches, dizziness, and irritability. Wear a wide-brimmed hat or stay in shade during peak sun hours (11 AM–3 PM).

Keep Your Feet Warm, Your Head Cool

This is one of TCM's most important summer principles. While it's tempting to go barefoot or wear sandals, cold feet can weaken kidney energy. Keep light socks on indoors while allowing your head and upper body to stay cool. This creates a balanced temperature gradient that supports overall health.

Practice Gentle Evening Exercise

Summer evenings are ideal for gentle movement. A 20–30 minute walk after dinner promotes digestion and allows the body to release accumulated heat through light perspiration. Tai Chi or gentle Qigong in the evening is also excellent — the slow movements calm the mind and prepare the body for restful sleep.

Stay Hydrated with Warm Water

This may seem counterintuitive, but TCM strongly recommends drinking warm or room-temperature water rather than ice-cold water. Warm water promotes circulation and is absorbed more efficiently by the body. Cold water constricts blood vessels and slows digestion. Keep a thermos of warm water with you throughout the day.

Summer Heat vs. Heat Stroke: When to Seek Help

TCM provides excellent guidance for preventing and managing mild to moderate summer heat discomfort. However, heat stroke is a medical emergency that requires immediate professional attention.

Seek Immediate Medical Help If You Experience:

Body temperature above 103°F (39.4°C), hot and red skin that stops sweating, rapid pulse, confusion or disorientation, nausea and vomiting, loss of consciousness, or seizures. These are signs of heat stroke, which can be life-threatening and requires emergency medical treatment.

TCM Prevention Is Key

The best approach is prevention. By following the dietary and lifestyle guidelines in this article — eating cooling foods, staying hydrated, avoiding excessive cold, and resting during peak heat — you can significantly reduce your risk of heat-related illness. If you do feel heat exhaustion symptoms (heavy sweating, weakness, cold clammy skin, nausea), move to a cool place, sip warm water, and press LI11 and PC6 acupressure points while resting.

Conclusion

Summer heat doesn't have to leave you exhausted and uncomfortable. By embracing the wisdom of Traditional Chinese Medicine — eating cooling foods that nourish rather than shock, using acupressure to restore balance, and aligning your daily rhythm with the season — you can stay cool, energized, and healthy throughout the summer months.

Remember, the TCM approach to summer cooling is not about fighting the heat — it's about working with your body's natural mechanisms to maintain balance. Start with one or two cooling soups, try the acupressure points when you feel overheated, and gradually build summer habits that support your long-term health. With these ancient practices, you'll find that summer becomes a season of vitality rather than exhaustion.

Quick Summer Relief Checklist

  • Sip chrysanthemum or honeysuckle tea throughout the day
  • Prepare mung bean soup 2–3 times per week
  • Press LI11 (elbow) and PC6 (wrist) acupressure points when overheated
  • Drink warm or room-temperature water — avoid ice-cold drinks
  • Rest during peak heat hours (11 AM–2 PM)
  • Eat light, steamed, or boiled meals instead of heavy fried foods
  • Walk gently in the evening to release heat through light perspiration
  • Wear a hat and stay in shade during midday sun
