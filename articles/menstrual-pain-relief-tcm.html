Menstrual Pain Relief with TCM: 5 Acupressure Points and Warming Remedies

📅 June 23, 2026 • Women's Health
Advertisement Space (728x90)

Menstrual pain, known as dysmenorrhea in medical terms, affects up to 80% of women at some point in their lives. While Western medicine often reaches for painkillers, Traditional Chinese Medicine (TCM) offers a gentler, root-cause approach that has helped women find relief for thousands of years. In TCM, period pain is not simply a "women's issue" to be endured — it's a signal that the body's energy (Qi) and blood flow need attention.

Whether you experience mild cramps that come and go, or debilitating pain that disrupts your daily life, TCM provides natural, effective strategies that address the underlying imbalances causing your discomfort. From acupressure points you can press at home to warming herbal teas and dietary changes, this guide offers practical solutions rooted in ancient Eastern wisdom.

Key Takeaways

  • TCM views menstrual pain as caused by Qi and blood stagnation, cold invasion, or organ deficiency
  • Five powerful acupressure points can provide natural period pain relief in minutes
  • Warming foods and herbal teas support the body's natural healing process during menstruation
  • Avoiding cold foods and keeping the lower abdomen warm prevents pain from worsening
  • Regular Qigong and stretching exercises promote smooth Qi and blood flow throughout the cycle

Understanding Menstrual Pain in TCM

In Western medicine, menstrual pain is typically attributed to prostaglandins — hormone-like substances that cause uterine contractions. While this explanation is valid, TCM takes a broader view, looking at the body's overall energy balance and organ function.

According to TCM theory, the liver, spleen, and kidneys play crucial roles in regulating the menstrual cycle. The Liver governs the smooth flow of Qi and blood; the Spleen produces blood and controls its movement within the vessels; and the Kidneys store essence (Jing) and govern reproduction. When any of these organs are imbalanced, the result can be pain during menstruation.

The Three Main Causes of Menstrual Pain in TCM

1. Qi and Blood Stagnation (气滞血瘀)

This is the most common cause of period pain. When Qi and blood become stuck or move too slowly, they create a "traffic jam" in the uterus. The resulting pain is often described as stabbing, fixed, or worsens with pressure. Women with this pattern may also experience breast tenderness, irritability, and dark menstrual blood with clots.

2. Cold Accumulation (寒凝血瘀)

Cold contracts and slows down circulation. When cold enters the uterus — from eating cold foods, wearing insufficient clothing, or exposure to cold environments — it causes the blood to congeal and flow sluggishly. The pain is often cramping in nature and improves with warmth. Women with this pattern may have a pale face, cold extremities, and dark menstrual blood.

3. Spleen and Kidney Deficiency (脾肾两虚)

When the Spleen and Kidneys are weak, the body cannot produce enough Qi and blood to nourish the reproductive organs. The pain is typically dull, dragging, and improves with rest and warmth. Women with this pattern may experience fatigue, dizziness, lower back soreness, and pale menstrual blood.

"When the Qi and blood flow smoothly, there is no pain. When they stagnate, pain arises."

— Traditional Chinese Medicine principle

5 Acupressure Points for Menstrual Pain Relief

Acupressure is one of the most accessible TCM techniques you can practice at home. By applying firm pressure to specific acupoints, you can stimulate the body's natural healing response, promote smooth Qi and blood flow, and relieve menstrual cramps within minutes.

1. Sanyinjiao (SP6) — The Three Yin Intersection

Location: Four finger-widths above the inner ankle bone, on the back edge of the shin bone.

This is arguably the most important acupressure point for women's health. SP6 is the meeting point of the Liver, Spleen, and Kidney meridians — three organs crucial for menstruation. Pressing this point nourishes blood, regulates menstruation, and relieves cramps. Apply firm pressure with your thumb for 2–3 minutes on each leg.

Note: Avoid this point during pregnancy, as it is known to stimulate uterine contractions.

2. Guanyuan (CV4) — The Gate of Origin

Location: Three finger-widths below the navel, on the midline of the lower abdomen.

CV4 is one of the most powerful points for warming the uterus and tonifying Kidney Qi. It directly influences the reproductive organs and is excellent for cold-type menstrual pain. Use the palm of your hand to gently massage this area in circular motions for 3–5 minutes. A warm compress placed here is also highly effective.

3. Zhongji (CV3) — The Central Pole

Location: Four finger-widths below the navel, directly below CV4.

CV3 is the front-Mu point of the Bladder and has a strong influence on the uterus. It helps regulate menstruation, relieve cramps, and address urinary issues that sometimes accompany periods. Press gently but firmly for 2 minutes, breathing deeply as you do so.

4. Taichong (LV3) — The Great Surge

Location: In the depression between the big toe and second toe, about two finger-widths from the webbing.

LV3 is the source point of the Liver meridian and is renowned for its ability to smooth the flow of Qi throughout the body. It's particularly effective for menstrual pain caused by emotional stress, irritability, or tension. Press firmly with your thumb for 2–3 minutes on each foot. You may feel a sensation of soreness — this indicates you've found the right spot.

5. Hegu (LI4) — Joining Valley

Location: In the webbing between the thumb and index finger, at the highest point of the muscle when thumb and index finger are brought together.

LI4 is one of the most commonly used acupressure points for pain relief in the entire body. It has a powerful analgesic effect and works particularly well for menstrual cramps when combined with SP6. Press firmly with the opposite hand's thumb for 2–3 minutes, using a kneading motion.

Note: Avoid this point during pregnancy.

How to Perform Acupressure for Period Pain

  • Timing: Begin pressing these points 1–2 days before your expected period, and continue throughout the first 2–3 days when pain is typically worst
  • Technique: Use the pad of your thumb or a knuckle to apply firm, steady pressure. You should feel a dull ache or soreness — this is the "de Qi" sensation that indicates the point is activated
  • Duration: Hold each point for 2–3 minutes, breathing deeply and slowly
  • Frequency: Repeat 3–5 times throughout the day as needed
  • Combination: For best results, combine acupressure with a warm compress on the lower abdomen

Warming Herbal Teas for Menstrual Relief

In TCM, warm beverages are essential during menstruation. They promote circulation, warm the uterus, and help expel cold and stagnation. Here are three traditional recipes that have been used for centuries to ease period discomfort.

1. Ginger and Brown Sugar Tea (红糖姜茶)

The classic Chinese period remedy. Fresh ginger warms the digestive system and promotes blood circulation, while brown sugar nourishes blood and provides quick energy. This simple tea is the go-to drink for millions of Chinese women during their periods.

Recipe: Slice 5–6 pieces of fresh ginger. Add to 2 cups of water and bring to a boil. Simmer for 10 minutes. Add 2 tablespoons of brown sugar. Stir until dissolved. Drink warm, 2–3 times daily during menstruation.

2. Red Date and Rose Bud Tea (红枣玫瑰花茶)

For blood nourishment and emotional calm. Red dates build blood, while rose buds gently move Qi and relieve emotional tension. This tea is especially helpful for women who experience irritability, mood swings, or breast tenderness along with their period pain.

Recipe: Add 5–6 red dates (pitted and halved) and 8–10 dried rose buds to a teapot. Pour boiling water over them and steep for 10 minutes. Drink throughout the day. Can be re-steeped 2–3 times.

3. Goji Berry and Longan Tea (枸杞龙眼茶)

For deficiency-type menstrual pain. This warming, nourishing tea is ideal for women whose period pain is dull, dragging, and accompanied by fatigue, dizziness, or pale menstrual blood. It builds blood, calms the mind, and supports kidney function.

Recipe: Add 1 tablespoon of goji berries and 8–10 longan fruits (dried or fresh) to a teapot. Pour boiling water over them and steep for 15 minutes. Add a small piece of rock sugar if desired. Drink warm, once daily.

Foods to Eat During Your Period

The right foods can significantly ease menstrual discomfort. TCM recommends warming, nourishing foods that promote circulation and support the body's natural healing process during menstruation.

Warm Soups and Stews

Hot soups made with chicken, bone broth, or vegetables provide warmth, hydration, and easy-to-digest nutrition. Adding ginger, turmeric, or black pepper enhances their warming properties.

Dark Leafy Greens

Spinach, kale, and dark leafy greens are rich in iron, magnesium, and B vitamins — all essential for healthy blood and reducing cramps. Cook them lightly with warming spices for the best effect.

Warm Fruits

While raw fruits are generally avoided during menstruation in TCM, cooked or stewed fruits like apples, pears, and berries are excellent choices. They provide vitamins and antioxidants without introducing cold into the body.

Ginger-Infused Foods

Adding fresh ginger to your cooking or tea during menstruation promotes circulation, warms the uterus, and relieves cramps. Ginger is considered one of the most important warming foods in TCM.

Foods to Avoid During Your Period

Cold and Icy Foods

No ice cream, cold drinks, ice water, or frozen foods. These introduce cold into the digestive system and uterus, worsening cramps and potentially causing blood to congeal.

Raw Salads and Cold Fruits

While healthy at other times, raw salads, cold fruits (watermelon, pears, citrus), and cold smoothies are best avoided during menstruation. Opt for cooked vegetables and warm fruits instead.

Caffeine and Alcohol

Coffee and alcohol constrict blood vessels, which can worsen cramps and increase tension. They also deplete the body of hydration and nutrients. Choose warm herbal teas instead.

Spicy and Greasy Foods

While warming foods are encouraged, excessively spicy or greasy foods can create internal heat and inflammation. Stick to gently warming foods like ginger and cinnamon rather than hot peppers or deep-fried foods.

Gentle Exercises for Menstrual Pain Relief

Movement is essential for promoting smooth Qi and blood flow during menstruation. However, the exercise should be gentle and appropriate for the body's state during this time.

Gentle Stretching

Simple yoga poses like Child's Pose, Cat-Cow, and Supine Twist help release tension in the lower abdomen and pelvis. Hold each pose for 30 seconds to 1 minute, breathing deeply and slowly. Avoid intense inversions or hot yoga during menstruation.

Walking

A 15–20 minute gentle walk outdoors promotes blood circulation, reduces stress, and can significantly ease menstrual cramps. Walking is especially beneficial if the weather is mild and you can stay warm.

Abdominal Massage

Using the palm of your hand, gently massage the lower abdomen in clockwise circles for 5–10 minutes. The warmth from your hands and the circular motion help promote blood flow and relieve cramping. For extra warmth, use a small amount of sesame oil or ginger oil.

Deep Breathing

Deep abdominal breathing activates the parasympathetic nervous system, reducing stress and relaxing the uterine muscles. Lie on your back with one hand on your chest and one on your belly. Breathe deeply through your nose, allowing your belly to rise. Exhale slowly through your mouth. Practice for 5–10 minutes.

Lifestyle Tips for Long-Term Menstrual Health

While acute relief is important, TCM also emphasizes prevention and long-term wellness. These lifestyle habits can help reduce menstrual pain over time.

Keep Warm Throughout the Month

Don't wait until your period starts to think about warmth. Keeping your feet, lower abdomen, and lower back warm throughout the month — especially in the 1–2 weeks before your period — helps prevent cold from accumulating in the uterus. Wear socks, use a heating pad, and avoid sitting on cold surfaces.

Manage Stress

In TCM, emotional stress directly impacts the Liver's ability to move Qi and blood smoothly. Regular stress management practices — meditation, journaling, time in nature, or simply setting boundaries — can significantly reduce menstrual pain over time.

Sleep Before Midnight

According to the TCM organ clock, the hours between 11 PM and 3 AM are when the Liver and Gallbladder regenerate and detoxify. Adequate sleep during these hours supports healthy blood production and smooth Qi flow, which directly impacts menstrual health.

Regular Qigong Practice

Gentle Qigong movements like "Separating Heaven and Earth" and "Two Hands Hold the Heavens" from the Baduanjin set promote smooth Qi and blood flow throughout the body. Practicing these exercises regularly throughout the month can reduce the severity of menstrual cramps over time.

When to Seek Professional Help

While TCM offers effective natural solutions for most menstrual discomfort, it's important to consult a healthcare professional if you experience:

  • Pain so severe that it interferes with daily activities, work, or school
  • Menstrual pain that suddenly worsens after years of relatively pain-free periods
  • Pain accompanied by fever, unusual discharge, or other concerning symptoms
  • Heavy bleeding that requires changing pads or tampons every hour
  • Pain that doesn't respond to home remedies after 2–3 cycles
  • Symptoms that suggest an underlying condition like endometriosis, fibroids, or PCOS

Important Note

The practices and remedies described in this article are based on Traditional Chinese Medicine principles and are intended for educational purposes. While generally safe, herbal teas and acupressure may not be appropriate for everyone, particularly pregnant women or those with certain medical conditions. Always consult with your healthcare provider before starting any new health regimen.

Conclusion

Menstrual pain doesn't have to be an inevitable part of life. TCM offers a comprehensive, natural approach that not only relieves acute discomfort but also addresses the root imbalances that cause pain in the first place. By combining acupressure, warming foods, gentle movement, and mindful lifestyle practices, you can transform your relationship with your menstrual cycle from one of dread to one of balance and wellness.

Remember, the goal of TCM is not just to eliminate symptoms — it's to create a state of balance where the body can heal itself. Start with one or two practices from this guide, and gradually build a routine that works for your unique body and lifestyle. With consistency and patience, you'll find that each cycle becomes easier than the last.

Quick Relief Checklist

  • Press SP6, CV4, and LV3 acupressure points for 2–3 minutes each
  • Drink ginger and brown sugar tea 2–3 times daily
  • Keep the lower abdomen warm with a heating pad or warm compress
  • Eat warm, cooked foods — avoid cold, raw, and icy items
  • Practice gentle stretching or walking for 15–20 minutes
  • Apply abdominal massage in clockwise circles for 5 minutes
  • Practice deep breathing to relax and reduce stress
  • Start these practices 1–2 days before your expected period
