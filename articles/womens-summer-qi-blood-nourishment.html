Women's Summer Qi & Blood Nourishment: 7 TCM Foods and Herbal Teas to Stay Energized

📅 June 26, 2026 • Women's Health
Advertisement Space (728x90)

Summer is a season of abundant energy — the sun is high, days are long, and nature is at its peak. But for many women, summer can also be a draining time. The heat makes you sweat more, weakens digestion, and depletes your Qi (vital energy) and Blood — the two most essential substances for women's health in Traditional Chinese Medicine (TCM). If you've ever felt unusually fatigued, light-headed, or pale during summer, it's not just the weather — your Qi and Blood may be calling for nourishment.

In TCM, women are governed by Blood (血, Xue). Unlike men, whose physiology centers on Qi, women's monthly cycles, pregnancy, childbirth, and breastfeeding all draw deeply on Blood reserves. Summer heat, with its dispersing nature, further consumes Qi and damages Yin, leaving many women depleted. The solution? Targeted nourishment with the right foods, herbal teas, and acupressure — all guided by centuries of Chinese medical wisdom.

In this guide, you'll discover seven TCM foods and herbal teas specifically chosen for women's Qi and Blood nourishment in summer, plus three powerful acupressure points to activate your body's energy channels. Every recommendation is gentle enough for daily use yet potent enough to make a measurable difference in your energy levels, skin radiance, and overall vitality.

Key Takeaways

  • Qi and Blood are the foundation of women's health in TCM — summer heat depletes both, so targeted nourishment is essential during hot months.
  • Animal-based foods build Blood fastest (liver, bone broth), while plant-based options (red dates, goji berries, black sesame) provide steady, gentle nourishment.
  • Herbal teas are summer's perfect delivery system — they hydrate, cool, and nourish simultaneously without burdening digestion.
  • Three key acupressure points — San Yin Jiao (SP6), Zu San Li (ST36), and Xue Hai (SP10) — can be pressed daily to stimulate Qi and Blood production.

Understanding Qi and Blood in Women's Health

In Traditional Chinese Medicine, Qi (气) is the vital force that animates every function of your body — it warms you, protects you from illness, transforms food into energy, and holds your organs in place. Blood (血) nourishes and moistens every tissue: your skin, hair, muscles, and internal organs. Together, Qi and Blood circulate through a network of channels (meridians), and when they are abundant and flowing smoothly, you feel energized, your complexion glows, and your menstrual cycle is regular and pain-free.

For women specifically, TCM teaches that "Qi generates Blood, and Blood carries Qi." This means the two are inseparable — weak Qi leads to weak Blood, and deficient Blood leaves Qi without a vessel to travel in. The signs of Qi and Blood deficiency in women include:

  • Chronic fatigue, especially in the afternoon
  • Pale complexion and pale lips
  • Light or irregular menstrual periods
  • Dizziness or light-headedness upon standing
  • Dry hair, brittle nails, and dull skin
  • Poor memory and difficulty concentrating
  • Heart palpitations and restless sleep

Summer amplifies these symptoms because heat is "dispersing" in nature — it pushes Qi outward toward the skin's surface, leaving the interior organs relatively depleted. Sweating further depletes both fluids and Qi. That's why smart summer nourishment for women focuses on gently building Blood while protecting Qi, using foods and herbs that are cooling or neutral in thermal nature — not hot or drying.

"Women are governed by Blood. When Blood is abundant, the body is nourished, the spirit is calm, and the complexion glows. When Blood is deficient, a hundred diseases arise." — Traditional Chinese Medicine principle

7 TCM Foods and Herbal Teas for Women's Summer Qi & Blood Nourishment

1. Red Dates (Jujube, 红枣) — The Queen of Blood Tonics

Red dates are perhaps the most famous Blood-nourishing food in all of TCM. They are sweet, warm, and enter the Spleen and Stomach meridians — the very organs responsible for transforming food into Qi and Blood. Rich in iron, vitamin C, and B-complex vitamins, red dates are a gentle daily tonic suitable for almost every woman. In summer, eat them sparingly (3-5 pieces per day) to avoid overheating, or better yet, steep them in cool water overnight for a refreshing infusion.

2. Goji Berries (Wolfberry, 枸杞子) — Blood and Yin Dual Tonic

Goji berries are one of the few foods in TCM that nourish both Blood and Yin simultaneously — making them perfect for summer when heat damages Yin. They are neutral in thermal nature, meaning they won't add heat to the body, and they specifically target the Liver and Kidney meridians, which are central to women's reproductive health. Add a small handful to tea, oatmeal, or soup. Modern research confirms goji berries are rich in zeaxanthin (for eye health), polysaccharides (for immune function), and iron.

3. Black Sesame Seeds (黑芝麻) — Deep Nourishment for Hair and Skin

Black sesame seeds are a premier Yin and Blood tonic in TCM, with a special affinity for the Liver and Kidneys. They are rich in calcium, iron, magnesium, and vitamin E — making them outstanding for women's hair health, skin radiance, and bone strength. Lightly toast a tablespoon of black sesame seeds and sprinkle over rice, congee, or yogurt. For maximum absorption, grind them before eating to break the tough outer hull.

4. Organic Chicken or Beef Liver (动物肝脏) — The Fastest Blood Builder

In TCM, animal liver is considered the most direct Blood-nourishing food because it goes straight to the Liver meridian. It is exceptionally rich in heme iron (the most absorbable form), vitamin B12, and folate — the exact nutrients needed for red blood cell production. For women with anemia, heavy periods, or postpartum recovery, 60-90 grams of organic liver once a week can make a dramatic difference. In summer, pair liver with cooling vegetables like spinach or celery to balance its warming nature.

5. Eight Treasures Tea (八宝茶) — The Complete Women's Tonic

Eight Treasures Tea is a beloved tradition in Chinese households, especially for women. The classic blend combines red dates, goji berries, longan (dried longan fruit, 桂圆), rock sugar, chrysanthemum flowers, Chinese Wolfberry leaves, raisins, and green tea. Longan is a particularly prized addition — it nourishes both Blood and the Heart, calming the spirit and improving sleep quality. Drink this tea warm (not hot) in the mornings for a steady release of energy throughout the day.

6. Dang Gui and Red Date Tea (当归红枣茶) — The Classic Blood Tonic

Dang Gui (Angelica sinensis, 当归) is known as "women's ginseng" for good reason — it is the single most important herb for regulating women's Blood in TCM. It moves and nourishes Blood simultaneously, making it effective for period pain, irregular cycles, and postpartum recovery. Combined with red dates and a slice of ginger, it becomes a deeply warming yet gentle tonic. Important note: avoid Dang Gui during heavy menstrual flow or if you are pregnant, as its Blood-moving properties can be counterproductive in these situations.

7. Longan and Lotus Seed Porridge (桂圆莲子粥) — Calm the Spirit, Nourish the Blood

This classic TCM recipe is less a tea and more a light summer congee — perfect for breakfast or a late dinner. Dried longan nourishes Blood and calms the Heart (Shen), while lotus seeds (莲子) strengthen the Spleen and stop any tendency toward loose stools — a common summer digestive pattern. Cook both ingredients with white rice and a small piece of dried tangerine peel (陈皮) to prevent cloying. The result is a mildly sweet, deeply comforting porridge that builds Blood without heaviness.

Important: When to Avoid Blood Tonics

Blood-nourishing foods are not suitable for everyone at all times. If you have a cold or flu with fever, avoid blood tonics — they can "trap" the pathogen inside. If you have digestive dampness (bloating, loose stools, greasy tongue coating), focus on clearing dampness first before building Blood. During heavy menstrual flow, pause Blood-moving herbs like Dang Gui. If you are pregnant, always consult a qualified TCM practitioner before using any herbal formulas.

3 Powerful Acupressure Points for Qi and Blood

Acupressure is a free, always-available tool you can use to support your Qi and Blood circulation. These three points are selected specifically for women's health and can be pressed daily for 1-2 minutes each.

San Yin Jiao (三阴交, SP6) — The Most Important Point for Women's Health

San Yin Jiao translates to "Three Yin Intersection" because it is the meeting point of the Liver, Spleen, and Kidney meridians — the three Yin channels most critical for women's gynecological health. This point regulates menstruation, strengthens the Spleen (which produces Blood), and nourishes the Kidneys. Press firmly with your thumb for 1-2 minutes on each leg. Best time: any time of day, but avoid during pregnancy (this point can stimulate uterine contractions).

📍 Found four finger-widths above the inner ankle bone, just behind the tibia (shin bone).

Zu San Li (足三里, ST36) — The Great Qi Tonifier

Zu San Li is one of the most famous acupoints in all of TCM, often called "the point of longevity." Located on the Stomach meridian, it directly strengthens the digestive system's ability to extract Qi and Blood from food. This point is especially valuable in summer when digestion tends to be weaker. Press deeply with a circular motion for 2 minutes on each leg. Some practitioners recommend pressing this point 36 times clockwise each morning — a practice associated with a robust immune system and long life.

📍 Found four finger-widths below the kneecap, one finger-width to the outside of the shin bone.

Xue Hai (血海, SP10) — The Sea of Blood

Xue Hai literally means "Sea of Blood" — it is the point on the Spleen meridian that directly regulates Blood, making it essential for any Blood-related women's health concern: scanty periods, irregular cycles, or skin issues related to Blood heat. Press this point with your thumb for 1-2 minutes on each leg. Best time: press daily for general maintenance, or 2-3 times daily during the week before your period for menstrual regulation.

📍 With your knee slightly bent, place your palm on the knee with your thumb pointing up — the point is where your thumb tip naturally lands, about two finger-widths above the inner upper border of the kneecap.

A Sample Summer Day of Qi and Blood Nourishment

Morning (7:00 AM – 9:00 AM) — Stomach meridian is most active

Start the day with a small bowl of Longan and Lotus Seed Porridge (recipe #7). Follow with a cup of Eight Treasures Tea (recipe #5) as you ease into your morning. Press Zu San Li (ST36) for two minutes on each leg before breakfast to prime your digestive fire.

Midday (11:00 AM – 1:00 PM) — Heart meridian and midday rest

Lunch should be your most substantial meal. Include a small portion of stir-fried organic liver with spinach (recipe #4) or, if you prefer plant-based, a red date and goji berry salad with toasted black sesame seeds (recipes #1, #2, #3). Rest for 15 minutes after eating — even a short horizontal rest in summer helps Blood return to the Liver for storage and detoxification.

Afternoon (3:00 PM – 5:00 PM) — Bladder meridian, natural energy dip

This is when Qi naturally dips. Brew a cup of Dang Gui and Red Date Tea (recipe #6) and take five minutes to press San Yin Jiao (SP6) on both legs. Avoid reaching for coffee or sugar — these provide a temporary lift but ultimately deplete Qi further.

Evening (9:00 PM – 11:00 PM) — San Jiao meridian, preparation for sleep

Finish your day with a few sips of warm water and press Xue Hai (SP10) for two minutes on each leg. Blood returns to the Liver during sleep for regeneration — going to bed by 11:00 PM maximizes this natural repair process. Avoid heavy meals, screens, and stimulating conversations in the hour before bed.

Lifestyle Habits That Protect Qi and Blood in Summer

Food and herbs alone cannot build Qi and Blood if your lifestyle constantly depletes them. Here are five essential summer habits to protect your reserves:

  • Protect your middle from cold: In TCM, the Spleen and Stomach prefer warmth. Ice-cold drinks, smoothies, and raw salads in excess damage the digestive fire (Spleen Yang) that transforms food into Qi and Blood. Enjoy summer foods at room temperature, or add a slice of ginger to your drinks to offset any cold.
  • Avoid over-sweating: While a moderate sweat during exercise is healthy, excessive sweating "leaks" both Qi and Blood. In summer, choose gentler forms of movement — brisk walking, Tai Chi, swimming, or restorative yoga — rather than high-intensity workouts under the midday sun.
  • Honor the midday rest: TCM's organ clock teaches that 11:00 AM – 1:00 PM is governed by the Heart meridian, making a short rest at this time especially restorative for Blood. Even 10-15 minutes of quiet sitting or lying down can make a noticeable difference.
  • Stay emotionally balanced: In TCM, the Liver stores Blood, and anger or frustration stagnates Liver Qi, which in turn disrupts Blood flow. Summer's heat can stir irritability — practices like deep abdominal breathing, journaling, or a cooling foot soak in the evening help settle the mind.
  • Sleep before 11:00 PM: According to TCM's organ clock, Blood returns to the Liver for detoxification and regeneration between 11:00 PM and 3:00 AM. If you are awake during these hours, the Liver cannot properly store and renew Blood, accelerating deficiency over time.
"To nourish Blood, first protect the Spleen. To move Blood, first soothe the Liver. When these two organs are in harmony, a woman's health naturally flourishes." — Traditional Chinese Medicine proverb

Frequently Asked Questions About Qi and Blood Nourishment

How long does it take to build Qi and Blood with TCM?

Building Blood takes patience. While some women notice improved energy within 1-2 weeks of consistent dietary changes, full replenishment of Blood stores can take 3-6 months. This is because red blood cells have a lifespan of about 120 days, and the body's production system needs time to ramp up. Consistency with daily small amounts is far more effective than occasional large doses.

Can I combine these foods with Western supplements like iron?

Yes, but with one important distinction: Western iron supplements are often constipating and can generate internal heat (a TCM pattern called "Stomach Heat"). If you are taking iron supplements, consume them alongside TCM Blood-nourishing foods that are cooling and moistening — like goji berries, black sesame, and adequate hydration — to balance this side effect. Always space iron supplements away from tea, as tannins inhibit iron absorption.

Are there any foods I should avoid while building Blood in summer?

Yes. Minimize or avoid: iced beverages and frozen desserts (damage Spleen Yang and impair Blood production), excessive raw salads and cold foods (same mechanism), spicy, deep-fried foods (generate internal heat and dry out Blood), and alcohol (generates Damp-Heat, which obstructs the Spleen). In TCM thinking, what you avoid is sometimes more important than what you add.
