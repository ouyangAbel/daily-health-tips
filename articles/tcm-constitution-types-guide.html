TCM Body Constitution Types: Discover Your 中医体质 & Personalized Health Guide

📏 July 2, 2026 • TCM Fundamentals
Advertisement Space (728x90)

🔑 Key Takeaway

Traditional Chinese Medicine recognizes 9 distinct body constitution types (中医体质), each with unique physical traits, emotional tendencies, and health vulnerabilities. Understanding your constitution is the foundation of personalized TCM healthcare — guiding everything from diet and exercise to herbal remedies and lifestyle adjustments.

Have you ever wondered why two people can eat the same food, experience the same weather, or face similar stress — yet react completely differently? In Western medicine, this is often attributed to genetics or "individual differences." But Traditional Chinese Medicine (TCM) has a far more detailed and practical framework: the Nine Body Constitution Types (九种体质).

Developed over centuries by TCM scholars and formally systematized by Professor Wang Qi (王琦) at Beijing University of Chinese Medicine, the constitution theory provides a personalized map of your body's strengths and vulnerabilities. It's one of the most powerful tools in TCM for preventive health (治未病) — treating illness before it manifests.

What Is Body Constitution (体质) in TCM?

In TCM, your constitution (体质 Tǐzhì) is your body's inherent pattern of physiological function, formed by a combination of congenital factors (what you're born with) and acquired influences (diet, lifestyle, environment, emotions). It determines:

  • Your natural energy levels — are you typically energetic or easily fatigued?
  • Your temperature tendencies — do you run hot or cold, prefer warmth or coolness?
  • Your digestive patterns — strong appetite or easily upset stomach?
  • Your emotional baseline — calm and steady, or prone to worry or irritability?
  • Your disease susceptibility — what conditions are you most vulnerable to?

Importantly, your constitution is not fixed. Through diet, exercise, herbs, and lifestyle adjustments, you can gradually shift an imbalanced constitution toward the ideal: the balanced, healthy type known as 平和质 (Pínghé Zhì).

The nine types fall into two groups: one balanced type (the ideal) and eight imbalanced types. Use the table below as a quick reference, then dive deeper into each type below.

Deep Dive: Each TCM Constitution Explained

1. Balanced Constitution — 平和质 (Pínghé Zhì)

This is the gold standard — the constitution everyone should aim for. People with balanced constitution have abundant Qi and Blood (气血充足), strong digestion, regular bowel movements, clear skin, a calm and optimistic disposition, and excellent sleep. They adapt well to environmental changes and rarely fall ill. Only about 32% of the population has a purely balanced constitution.

🍽️ Maintenance Diet for Balanced Types

Eat a wide variety of foods across all five flavors (sour, bitter, sweet, pungent, salty). Include plenty of seasonal vegetables, moderate grains, and lean proteins. Green tea is excellent for daily maintenance. The key is moderation and variety — no need for special restrictions.

2. Qi Deficiency — 气虚质 (Qìxū Zhì)

Qi Deficiency is one of the most common imbalances in modern life, especially among office workers and those under chronic stress. The hallmark is persistent low energy (气不足). You may find yourself short of breath after mild activity, with a soft, weak voice, spontaneous sweating during the day, and a pale, puffy tongue with teeth marks on the edges. The Spleen and Lung are the organs most commonly involved — the Spleen fails to produce enough Qi, and the Lung fails to distribute it.

🍽️ Qi-Building Foods

Best choices: sweet rice (糯米), yam (山药 Shānyào), red dates (红枣 Hóngzǎo), astragalus root (黄芪 Huángqí), chicken, mushrooms (especially shiitake), and honey. Avoid: raw, cold foods and excessive bitter flavors which further deplete Qi. Cook foods well; soups and congees are ideal.

📍 Best Acupoint: Zusanli — 足三里 (ST36)

Located four finger-widths below the kneecap, one finger-width lateral to the shin bone. This is the #1 point for tonifying Qi and strengthening the Spleen-Stomach. Press firmly for 2 minutes on each leg daily. Moxibustion on this point is especially powerful for Qi Deficiency types.

3. Yang Deficiency — 阳虚质 (Yángxū Zhì)

Yang Deficiency types are perpetually cold. They wear more layers than others, have cold hands and feet even in summer, prefer hot drinks, and may have a pale, puffy body with loose stools. The lower back and knees often feel weak and cold. This relates to Kidney Yang insufficiency (肾阳虚) — the body's pilot light of metabolic fire is dimmed. Cold weather and raw foods make symptoms worse.

🍽️ Yang-Warming Foods

Best choices: lamb, ginger (生姜), cinnamon (肉桂 Ròuguì), walnuts, black pepper, fennel, and shrimp. Cook with warming spices. Avoid: cold drinks, ice cream, raw salads, watermelon, and cold-natured fruits like pears and bananas.

📍 Best Acupoint: Mingmen — 命门 (GV4 / Du 4)

Located on the lower back, directly opposite the navel, in the depression below the spinous process of L2. This is the Gate of Life — the source of the body's Yang fire. Apply moxibustion (艾灸) here for 10–15 minutes daily, or place a hot water bottle on the area while resting.

4. Yin Deficiency — 阴虚质 (Yīnxū Zhì)

Where Yang Deficiency means too little warmth, Yin Deficiency means too little moisture and cooling capacity. These individuals feel warm — especially in the palms, soles, and chest (五心烦热) — have dry mouth and throat, prefer cold drinks, suffer from night sweats, and may have a thin body type with dry skin and constipation. The tongue is red with little or no coating. Kidney and Lung Yin are the most common areas affected, often worsened by late nights, overwork, and spicy foods.

🍽️ Yin-Nourishing Foods

Best choices: pears (梨), lily bulb (百合 Bǎihé), black sesame, goji berries (枸杞子 Gǒuqǐzǐ), duck meat, tofu, seaweed, honey, and tremella mushroom (银耳). Soups and stewed dishes are best. Avoid: spicy peppers, alcohol, fried foods, and late-night eating.

5. Phlegm-Dampness — 痰湿质 (Tánshī Zhì)

Phlegm-Dampness types tend toward overweight and sluggishness. They have a soft, puffy body, oily skin, a greasy tongue coating, a heavy sensation in the limbs, and often feel mentally foggy. They sweat easily and produce excessive phlegm or mucus. The root cause is Spleen deficiency leading to dampness accumulation (脾虚生湿) — the Spleen fails to transport and transform fluids, which then congeal into phlegm-dampness. Sedentary lifestyle and rich, greasy foods make this pattern worse.

🍽️ Dampness-Clearing Foods

Best choices: barley / Job's tears (薏米 Yìmǐ), adzuki beans (赤小豆), bitter melon (苦瓜), radish, lotus root, seaweed, and green tea. Avoid: dairy, sugar, greasy/fried foods, alcohol, and heavy meats. Regular light sweating through exercise is crucial for this type.

6. Damp-Heat — 湿热质 (Shīrè Zhì)

Damp-Heat types combine the heaviness of dampness with the agitation of heat. They tend to have oily, acne-prone skin, a bitter or sticky taste in the mouth, yellow tongue coating, and dark, scanty urine. They may feel irritable and uncomfortable in humid weather. This pattern often arises from long-term consumption of alcohol, spicy-greasy foods, and living in humid environments — the Spleen and Liver are typically involved.

🍽️ Damp-Heat Clearing Foods

Best choices: mung beans (绿豆), cucumber, watermelon, dandelion greens (蒲公英), lotus leaf tea (荷叶茶), bitter melon, and millet. Avoid: alcohol, spicy food, barbecue, mango, and durian — all of which worsen damp-heat.

7. Blood Stasis — 血瘀质 (Xuèyū Zhì)

Blood Stasis types have poor circulation. They often have a dark or dull complexion, dark circles under the eyes, purplish lips, spider veins or varicosities, and a tongue that is dark purple with stagnant spots. They bruise easily and experience fixed, stabbing pains. Women may have severe menstrual cramps with dark, clotted blood. Chronic stress, injuries, or prolonged Qi Deficiency can all lead to Blood Stasis — where Blood fails to flow smoothly (血行不畅).

🍽️ Blood-Moving Foods

Best choices: hawthorn berry (山楂 Shānzhā), black fungus (黑木耳), turmeric, saffron (a few strands in warm water), brown sugar, and moderate red wine. Vinegar-based dressings help move blood. Avoid: excessively fatty foods that thicken the blood.

8. Qi Stagnation — 气郁质 (Qìyù Zhì)

Qi Stagnation types are emotionally sensitive. They tend toward anxiety, depression, mood swings, and frequent sighing. Physically, they may experience a sensation of a lump in the throat (梅核气), chest or rib-side distension, and digestive issues triggered by stress. The Liver is the key organ here — Liver Qi Stagnation (肝气郁结) drives the whole pattern. This constitution is increasingly common in high-stress modern life.

🍵 Qi-Smoothing Tea: Rose & Tangerine Peel

Steep 3–5 dried rose buds (玫瑰花) and a small piece of dried tangerine peel (陈皮 Chénpí) in hot water for 5 minutes. Rose moves Liver Qi and calms the spirit; tangerine peel regulates Qi and harmonizes the middle. Drink mid-morning or after stressful events. This is one of the gentlest and most effective remedies for Qi Stagnation.

📍 Best Acupoint: Taichong — 太冲 (LR3)

Located on the top of the foot, in the depression between the first and second metatarsal bones. This is the primary point for moving Liver Qi. Press firmly with your thumb for 1–2 minutes on each foot. You'll know you've found it if it feels tender — this tenderness indicates stagnation.

9. Intrinsic / Allergic — 特禀质 (Tèbǐng Zhì)

The Intrinsic (or "Special Endowment") constitution covers people with congenital sensitivities and allergies. These individuals react strongly to environmental triggers — pollen, dust mites, certain foods, and temperature changes — with sneezing, rashes, asthma, or eczema. The root is a combination of congenital weakness of the Lung and Kidney defensive systems (肺肾不足). This type requires the most careful environmental management.

🍽️ Defensive-Strengthening Foods

Best choices: astragalus root (黄芪), fresh ginger, jujube dates, schisandra berries (五味子 Wǔwèizǐ), and lotus seeds. Avoid: known allergens, shellfish, mango, and "fa wu" (发物) — foods that are known to trigger allergic or inflammatory reactions in sensitive individuals, such as certain seafood, mushrooms, and spicy dishes.

"Knowing your constitution is like having a map of your body's terrain. When you understand the landscape, you know which paths lead to health and which lead to illness." — Professor Wang Qi (王琦), pioneer of TCM Constitution Theory

How to Identify Your Constitution

Determining your constitution is best done through a combination of self-observation and professional TCM diagnosis. The standardized Constitution in Chinese Medicine Questionnaire (CCMQ) — developed by Professor Wang Qi's team — consists of 60 questions and is used in TCM clinics worldwide. However, you can start with these self-assessment clues:

  • Temperature preference: Are you always cold? (→ Yang Deficiency) Always hot? (→ Yin Deficiency or Damp-Heat)
  • Energy pattern: Do you tire easily with mild activity? (→ Qi Deficiency) Feel heavy and sluggish? (→ Phlegm-Dampness)
  • Skin appearance: Oily and acne-prone? (→ Damp-Heat) Dry and flaky? (→ Yin Deficiency) Dull and dark? (→ Blood Stasis)
  • Emotional pattern: Anxious with frequent sighing? (→ Qi Stagnation) Irritable and hot-tempered? (→ Damp-Heat or Yin Deficiency)
  • Tongue examination: Pale and puffy with teeth marks? (→ Qi Deficiency) Red with little coating? (→ Yin Deficiency) Purple with spots? (→ Blood Stasis)
  • Allergies: Seasonal allergies, asthma, or eczema since childhood? (→ Intrinsic type)

Important note: Most people have a mixed constitution — a primary type with one or two secondary tendencies. For example, someone might be primarily Qi Deficiency with some Dampness. A qualified TCM practitioner can help you untangle these patterns.

Why Constitution Theory Matters for Health

The constitution framework transforms TCM from a purely illness-treating medicine to a truly preventive (预防为主) one. Here's why it's so powerful:

  • Personalized diet: Instead of generic "healthy eating," you eat specifically for YOUR body type
  • Targeted exercise: Yang Deficiency types benefit from gentle, warming movement (Tai Chi, Qigong); Phlegm-Dampness types need vigorous, sweat-inducing exercise
  • Disease prediction: Phlegm-Dampness types are at higher risk for metabolic syndrome and diabetes; Blood Stasis types for cardiovascular disease — knowing your constitution lets you intervene early
  • Seasonal adaptation: Your constitution determines how you should adjust diet and lifestyle each season

FAQ: Common Questions About TCM Constitution

Q: Can my constitution change over time?
Yes, absolutely. While you're born with certain tendencies, your constitution is heavily influenced by diet, lifestyle, environment, emotional state, and age. Many people shift from a balanced constitution toward Qi Deficiency in middle age, or toward Yin Deficiency after prolonged stress. The good news is that with consistent TCM practices, you can shift back toward balance.

Q: Can I have more than one constitution type?
Yes — mixed constitutions are actually the norm, not the exception. A common combination is Qi Deficiency with Dampness (the weak Spleen fails to process fluids), or Yin Deficiency with Blood Stasis (dryness leads to poor circulation). A TCM practitioner can help you prioritize which pattern to address first.

Q: How long does it take to rebalance a constitution?
Constitution change is gradual — think months to years, not days. Mild Qi Deficiency may respond well within 3–6 months of consistent diet and lifestyle changes. Deep-rooted Phlegm-Dampness or Blood Stasis may take longer. The key is consistency (坚持), not speed.

Q: Is this scientifically validated?
Yes. The CCMQ has been validated in large-scale studies involving thousands of participants across China. Research has linked specific constitution types to genetic markers, gut microbiome patterns, and metabolic profiles. TCM constitution theory is increasingly integrated into public health programs in China for disease prevention.

Your Next Steps

Understanding your 中医体质 is the first — and perhaps most important — step in your TCM health journey. Start by observing your body's signals with the self-assessment clues above. If you see a clear pattern, begin incorporating the appropriate foods and acupoints recommended for that type. For a comprehensive evaluation, seek out a qualified TCM practitioner who can perform tongue and pulse diagnosis alongside the CCMQ questionnaire.

Remember: your constitution is not a life sentence — it's a guide. Every day, through the choices you make around food, movement, rest, and emotional care, you are actively reshaping your inner landscape toward balance.
