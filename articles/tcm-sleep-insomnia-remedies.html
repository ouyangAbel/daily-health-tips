TCM Sleep Guide: Ancient Remedies to Cure Insomnia & Restore Restful Sleep Naturally

📅 July 04, 2026 • Sleep Health
Advertisement Space (728x90)

Sleep is not merely the absence of wakefulness. In Traditional Chinese Medicine (TCM), sleep is understood as a profound physiological event — the moment when Wei Qi (卫气, defensive energy) retreats from the surface of the body into the interior, when Yang energy submérges into Yin, and when the Hun (魂, ethereal soul) anchored in the Liver settles into the Blood for its nightly restoration. When any part of this delicate choreography is disrupted, insomnia (失眠, Shī Mián) takes hold.

Modern sleep medicine often treats insomnia as a single condition — a malfunction to be corrected with sedatives or sleep hygiene checklists. TCM, by contrast, recognizes that difficulty falling asleep, waking at specific hours of the night, dream-disturbed sleep, and early-morning waking each tell a different story about which organ system is out of balance. This guide draws on over two millennia of clinical wisdom to help you decode your insomnia pattern, apply targeted acupressure and herbal remedies, and align your daily rhythm with the TCM body clock (子午流注, Zǐ Wǔ Liú Zhù) for deep, restorative sleep.

Key Takeaways

  • Insomnia in TCM is never a single diagnosis — it reflects specific organ imbalances: Liver Qi Stagnation, Heart Fire, Spleen Deficiency, or Kidney Yin Deficiency, each with distinct symptoms and waking patterns
  • The TCM body clock (Chinese circadian rhythm) maps each two-hour interval to a specific organ; waking at the same time every night reveals which meridian needs attention
  • Five powerful acupressure points — including Shenmen HT7, Anmian (Peaceful Sleep), and Yin Tang — can be self-applied nightly to shorten sleep onset and reduce nighttime awakenings
  • Chinese herbal sleep formulas like Suanzaoren Tang (Sour Jujube Decoction) and single herbs like Schisandra berry (Wu Wei Zi) have centuries of clinical backing and growing modern research support
  • Ancient bedtime rituals — foot soaking, abdominal breathing, and evening Yin-nourishing foods — work synergistically to signal your nervous system that it is safe to descend into rest

How TCM Understands Sleep: The Dance of Yin, Yang, and Wei Qi

To understand why TCM treats insomnia differently from conventional medicine, you must first grasp the daily rhythm of Yin and Yang. During the day, Yang energy — active, warming, outward-moving — dominates. Wei Qi (defensive Qi) circulates on the body's surface, protecting against external pathogens and keeping the senses alert. As evening approaches, Yang gradually recedes and Yin — passive, cooling, inward-moving — takes over. Wei Qi withdraws from the exterior and sinks inward to nourish the internal organs. This is the TCM description of falling asleep.

Insomnia occurs when this transition fails. The most common reason, articulated in the Huangdi Neijing (黄帝内经) over 2,000 years ago, is that Yang cannot enter Yin — the active daytime energy remains trapped on the surface, leaving the mind racing, the body restless, and sleep elusive. But why does Yang fail to enter Yin? The answer depends on which organ system is affected:

1. Liver Qi Stagnation Transforming into Fire (肝郁化火)

This is the classic stress-induced insomnia pattern. The Liver is responsible for the smooth flow of Qi throughout the body. Chronic stress, frustration, and unexpressed anger cause Liver Qi to stagnate. Over time, this stagnation generates internal Heat and Fire that rise upward to disturb the Heart (which houses the Shen, or spirit). The result: difficulty falling asleep, irritability, a bitter taste in the mouth, and dreams filled with conflict or anger. You may lie in bed for hours, physically exhausted but mentally wired — this is Liver Fire keeping Yang trapped at the surface.

2. Heart and Spleen Deficiency (心脾两虚)

In TCM, the Spleen produces Blood from the food we eat, and the Heart governs Blood circulation. When overwork, worry, or poor diet weaken the Spleen, Blood production declines and the Heart is inadequately nourished. Without sufficient Blood to anchor the Shen (spirit), the mind becomes unmoored at night. This pattern produces a specific kind of insomnia: you fall asleep easily but wake repeatedly throughout the night, often with palpitations, poor memory, and a pale complexion. This is the insomnia of the overworked, the anxious, and those who burn the candle at both ends.

3. Yin Deficiency with Hyperactive Fire (阴虚火旺)

The Kidneys store the body's fundamental Yin and Yang. When Kidney Yin is depleted — through aging, chronic illness, overwork, or excessive sexual activity — there is insufficient cooling, anchoring energy to contain the body's Yang. Yang floats upward unchecked, producing the characteristic "deficiency heat" pattern: night sweats, hot palms and soles, a dry mouth at night, and a specific form of insomnia where you wake in the early hours (3–5 AM) and cannot return to sleep. This pattern is particularly common in perimenopausal and postmenopausal women.

4. Heart and Gallbladder Qi Deficiency (心胆气虚)

This pattern affects people who are constitutionally timid, easily startled, or recovering from a frightening experience. In TCM, the Gallbladder governs courage and decisiveness. When Gallbladder Qi is deficient, the Heart spirit becomes restless and easily frightened. The hallmark of this insomnia pattern: you feel sleepy but are afraid to fall asleep, startle awake at the slightest sound, and have vivid, frightening dreams. This is the insomnia of anxiety, PTSD, and deep-seated insecurity.

"When Wei Qi travels in the Yang, one is awake. When Wei Qi travels in the Yin, one sleeps. If the harmony between Yin and Yang is lost, sleep becomes disordered."

— Ling Shu (灵枢), the Spiritual Pivot, chapter on Wei Qi circulation

The TCM Body Clock: Why You Wake at the Same Time Every Night

One of the most clinically useful tools in TCM sleep medicine is the Chinese Organ Clock (子午流注, Zǐ Wǔ Liú Zhù — literally "Midnight-Noon Ebb-Flow"). This ancient system maps the 24-hour day into twelve two-hour intervals, with each interval governed by a specific organ meridian when its Qi is at peak circulation. If you consistently wake at the same time every night, the Clock tells you which organ needs support.

9 PM – 11 PM (San Jiao / Triple Burner 三焦经)

This is the ideal window to begin winding down. The San Jiao regulates the body's fluid metabolism and the balance between the upper, middle, and lower body cavities. If you feel restless or anxious during this window, it suggests an imbalance in the body's thermostat — often related to endocrine or metabolic dysregulation. This is the time for gentle yin activities: reading, meditation, foot soaking. If you habitually push through this window with screen time or late work, you are fighting your body's natural descent into Yin.

11 PM – 1 AM (Gallbladder 胆经)

The Gallbladder is the only organ in TCM classified as both a Fu (hollow) organ and an "Extraordinary Organ" because it stores bile — a "pure" substance rather than waste. During this window, the Gallbladder processes fats and supports decision-making and courage (in TCM psychology). Waking during this time often indicates Gallbladder Qi deficiency or Liver-Gallbladder disharmony, often linked to difficulty making decisions, resentment, or dietary excess. This is the most critical window to be asleep, as Gallbladder Qi supports the Liver's detoxification work in the following period.

1 AM – 3 AM (Liver 肝经)

This is the Liver's peak detoxification and Blood-cleansing window. In TCM, the Liver stores Blood during rest and releases it during activity. Waking consistently between 1–3 AM is one of the most common insomnia patterns and almost always points to Liver Qi Stagnation or Liver Fire. Emotionally, this corresponds to unresolved anger, frustration, or resentment. Physiologically, it may reflect the liver's struggle to process alcohol, medications, or metabolic waste. If this is your waking window, reducing alcohol intake, practicing emotional release work, and using Liver-soothing herbs (like Chrysanthemum or Bupleurum) can be transformative.

3 AM – 5 AM (Lung 肺经)

The Lung governs Qi and is the "tender organ" most vulnerable to external pathogens. Waking during this window — especially with coughing, sweating, or a feeling of grief or sadness — suggests Lung Qi deficiency or Lung Yin deficiency. In TCM psychology, the Lung houses the Po (魄), the corporeal soul associated with instinct and grief. People processing loss, whether recent or buried, frequently wake during these hours. Deep breathing exercises, and herbs that moisten the Lung (like Ophiopogon or Loquat leaf), are indicated.

5 AM – 7 AM (Large Intestine 大肠经)

This is the body's natural elimination window — ideally, you should wake and have a bowel movement during this period. Waking consistently at 5 AM with an urgent need to use the bathroom may indicate Large Intestine disharmony or Spleen Qi deficiency. However, waking at 5 AM feeling refreshed and ready to start the day is natural — this is Yang energy beginning its ascent. The key distinction: if you feel rested at 5 AM, rise. If you feel exhausted and still wake, a deeper imbalance needs addressing.

5 Powerful Acupressure Points for Restful Sleep

Acupressure (穴位按压) is one of the safest and most accessible TCM tools for insomnia — no needles, no herbs, no cost. Each of the following points can be stimulated with firm, circular pressure for 1–3 minutes before bed. Consistency is key: perform this routine nightly for at least two weeks before evaluating its effectiveness.

1. Shenmen 神门 (HT7) — Spirit Gate

Location: On the wrist crease, in the depression on the ulnar (little-finger) side, just radial to the pisiform bone and the flexor carpi ulnaris tendon.

HT7 is the Yuan-Source point of the Heart meridian and arguably the single most important acupoint for sleep. Its name — "Spirit Gate" — reveals its function: it calms the Shen (spirit), settles the mind, and opens the gateway into rest. Stimulating HT7 reduces heart palpitations, quiets racing thoughts, and is particularly effective for the "can't turn off my brain" type of insomnia. How to press: Use your opposite thumb to apply firm, steady pressure. Breathe deeply and slowly for 2–3 minutes on each wrist. You may feel a dull ache or a spreading warmth — this is the desired sensation.

2. Anmian 安眠 (Peaceful Sleep) — Extra Point

Location: Behind the ear, midway between the Fengchi GB20 point (in the depression below the occipital bone) and Yiming (behind the earlobe), approximately one thumb-width posterior to the mastoid process.

Anmian is not a classical meridian point but an "extra point" (奇穴) whose name translates literally to "Peaceful Sleep." It was discovered and refined by modern TCM practitioners specifically for treating insomnia, and clinical studies have confirmed its effectiveness for both sleep onset latency (time to fall asleep) and sleep maintenance. How to press: Use both index or middle fingers to apply bilateral pressure. The point is often tender in people with chronic insomnia. Massage in small circles for 2 minutes.

3. Yin Tang 印堂 (EX-HN3) — Hall of Impression

Location: Midway between the inner ends of the two eyebrows, at the root of the nose — commonly called the "third eye" point.

Yin Tang is the premier calming point on the face. It directly calms the Shen, relieves anxiety, and is particularly useful for insomnia accompanied by frontal headaches, sinus congestion, or mental overstimulation from screen exposure. Many people find that gentle pressure here triggers an immediate sigh or a softening of facial tension — a sign that the parasympathetic nervous system is activating. How to press: Use your index or middle finger. Close your eyes. Apply gentle, steady pressure while visualizing the tension draining from your forehead. Continue for 1–2 minutes.

4. Yongquan 涌泉 (KI1) — Gushing Spring

Location: On the sole of the foot, in the depression that forms when you curl your toes — approximately at the junction of the anterior one-third and posterior two-thirds of the sole, between the second and third metatarsal bones.

KI1 is the first point on the Kidney meridian and the only meridian point on the sole of the foot. In TCM theory, stimulating Yongquan draws excess Yang energy down from the head (where it causes racing thoughts and headaches) and grounds it into the Earth through the feet. It is especially indicated for the Yin Deficiency / Hyperactive Fire pattern — night sweats, hot soles, and early-morning waking. How to press: Before bed, soak your feet in warm water for 10 minutes (see section below), then use your thumb to apply deep, rotating pressure to KI1 on each foot for 2–3 minutes. Follow with firm rubbing of the entire sole until it feels warm.

5. Sanyinjiao 三阴交 (SP6) — Three Yin Intersection

Location: On the inner side of the lower leg, four finger-widths (approximately 3 cun) above the tip of the medial malleolus (inner ankle bone), just behind the tibia.

SP6 is the meeting point of the three Yin meridians of the leg — Liver, Spleen, and Kidney — making it one of the most harmonizing points in the body. By nourishing Yin, tonifying Blood, and calming the Shen, SP6 addresses insomnia at its root, particularly for women with sleep disturbances related to menstruation, pregnancy, or menopause. Note: This point is contraindicated during pregnancy. How to press: Use firm, steady pressure on both legs simultaneously. You may feel a strong radiating sensation. Hold for 2–3 minutes.

"When the Heart is calm, the Shen is at peace, and sleep comes naturally. All sleep remedies — whether needles, herbs, or pressure — ultimately work by calming the Heart."

— Traditional Chinese Medicine principle

Herbal Teas and Formulas for Better Sleep

Chinese herbal medicine offers a sophisticated pharmacopeia for insomnia — not a single "sleep herb" but a nuanced system of formulas matched to your specific pattern. Below are the most clinically important herbal approaches, from gentle single-herb teas you can steep tonight to classical formulas that have treated insomnia for centuries.

1. Suanzaoren Tang (酸枣仁汤) — Sour Jujube Decoction

This is the most famous TCM sleep formula, first recorded in the Jin Gui Yao Lue (Essential Prescriptions from the Golden Cabinet, circa 200 CE) by Zhang Zhongjing, the Hippocrates of Chinese medicine. Its chief herb is Suanzaoren (酸枣仁, Ziziphus jujuba seed), which directly nourishes the Heart and Liver Blood while calming the Shen. Modern research has identified jujubosides — the active compounds in Suanzaoren — as having genuine sedative and anxiolytic effects mediated through GABA receptors. The full formula combines Suanzaoren with Anemarrhena (Zhi Mu), Poria (Fu Ling), Chuanxiong, and Licorice to address the underlying Liver Blood deficiency and deficiency heat. Preparation: Suanzaoren is typically decocted (boiled for 20–30 minutes) to release its active compounds, though powdered extracts and teas are available. For a simple home version, steep 10–15 grams of crushed Suanzaoren in hot water for 15 minutes and drink 1 hour before bed.

2. Wu Wei Zi (五味子) — Schisandra Berry Tea

Wu Wei Zi — whose name means "Five-Flavor Berry" because it contains all five TCM tastes (sour, bitter, sweet, pungent, salty) — is a premier adaptogenic sedative. In TCM, it astringes the Lung Qi, nourishes the Kidneys, and most importantly for sleep, calms the Shen by astringing scattered Heart Qi. It is uniquely suited for the insomnia pattern where you feel "scattered" — jumping from thought to thought, waking frequently, and feeling depleted the next day. Modern research confirms Schisandra's hepatoprotective, neuroprotective, and mild sedative properties. Preparation: Simmer 5–8 grams of dried Schisandra berries in 2 cups of water for 15 minutes. Strain and drink warm 30–60 minutes before bed. The taste is distinctly sour — you may add a small amount of honey.

3. Juhua (菊花) and Gouqizi (枸杞子) — Chrysanthemum and Goji Berry Tea

This simple two-herb combination is the go-to evening tea for the Liver Fire / Liver Yang Rising pattern — the person whose insomnia is driven by stress, screen-induced eye strain, irritability, and a sensation of heat in the head. Chrysanthemum (Ju Hua) clears Liver Heat and benefits the eyes; Goji berry (Gou Qi Zi) nourishes Liver and Kidney Yin to anchor the rising Yang. Together they clear the excess while nourishing the deficiency — a classic TCM strategy known as "simultaneous attack and supplementation." Preparation: Steep 5–6 chrysanthemum flowers and 1 tablespoon of goji berries in hot (not boiling) water for 5–7 minutes. Sip slowly in the hour before bed. This tea is gentle enough for nightly use.

4. He Huan Hua (合欢花) — Albizia Flower / Mimosa Tree Blossom

In TCM, He Huan Hua is known as the "Collective Happiness Flower" because of its specific ability to relieve emotional constraint — the kind of sadness, grief, or emotional numbness that keeps you awake at night replaying painful memories. It calms the Shen while gently moving stagnant Liver Qi, making it ideal for insomnia with an emotional component: heartbreak, loss, or chronic low-grade depression. Preparation: Steep 3–6 grams of dried Albizia flowers in hot water for 10 minutes. Its flavor is mild and slightly sweet. Combine with a few rose buds (Mei Gui Hua) for enhanced Liver Qi-moving effects.

⚠️ Important Herbal Safety Notes

  • Suanzaoren (Sour Jujube seed) is generally safe but may cause mild drowsiness the next morning — start with a lower dose to assess your sensitivity.
  • Schisandra is contraindicated in acute febrile illnesses and during the early stages of a cold or flu when the pathogen is still on the exterior.
  • Goji berries may interact with warfarin and other blood-thinning medications — consult your doctor if you are on anticoagulants.
  • Pregnant and breastfeeding women should consult a qualified TCM practitioner before using any herbal sleep formulas.
  • If you take prescription sleep medications, do not discontinue them abruptly. Use herbal teas as a complement under medical supervision, not as an immediate replacement.

Ancient Bedtime Rituals: The TCM Sleep Hygiene Protocol

Long before the term "sleep hygiene" entered the modern lexicon, TCM had developed a comprehensive set of evening practices designed to guide the body's Yang energy inward and prepare the Shen for rest. These are not quick fixes — they are lifestyle rituals that, practiced consistently, retrain your nervous system to recognize that nighttime means sleep.

1. Evening Foot Soaking (足浴) — The Gateway to Sleep

Of all TCM sleep rituals, foot soaking is arguably the most powerful and accessible. Immersing your feet in warm water (40–43°C / 104–109°F) for 15–20 minutes before bed triggers a cascade of sleep-promoting physiological changes: peripheral vasodilation draws blood away from the brain (calming racing thoughts), activates the parasympathetic nervous system, and directly stimulates the Kidney meridian's first point, KI1 Yongquan, on the soles. Add a handful of Epsom salts (for magnesium absorption) or 3–4 slices of fresh ginger (for warming Yang-deficient constitutions) to enhance the effect. Protocol: Soak for 15–20 minutes. The water should be hot but not scalding. Immediately after soaking, dry your feet, put on socks to retain the warmth, and go directly to bed — the window of drowsiness after foot soaking is approximately 20–30 minutes. Do not check your phone after soaking.

2. Abdominal Breathing (腹式呼吸) — Activating the Rest-and-Digest Response

Shallow chest breathing is a hallmark of the stressed, sympathetic-dominant state that keeps you awake. Abdominal (diaphragmatic) breathing reverses this: it stimulates the vagus nerve, lowers cortisol, and shifts the body into parasympathetic mode. In TCM terms, deep abdominal breathing guides Qi downward to the Dantian (丹田, the energy center below the navel), counteracting the upward rush of rebellious Qi that fuels insomnia. Protocol: Lie on your back with one hand on your belly. Inhale slowly through your nose for a count of 4, feeling your belly rise. Hold for 4. Exhale slowly through your mouth for a count of 6, feeling your belly fall. Repeat for 5–10 minutes. This can be done in bed and often leads directly into sleep.

3. The "No Screen" Hour and Yin-Nourishing Evening Foods

The hour before bed is, in TCM, the time when the body's Yang energy naturally begins its descent into Yin. Blue light from screens disrupts this descent by signaling "daytime" to the brain. But beyond the light itself, the content we consume — emails, news, social media — stirs the Shen and triggers Liver Qi Stagnation. Protocol: Set a hard boundary: no screens for 60 minutes before your target sleep time. Replace screen time with a printed book, gentle stretching, or listening to calming music. Simultaneously, if you are hungry, eat a small evening snack that nourishes Yin rather than Yang: a small bowl of warm oat porridge, a few walnuts with a cup of warm milk (or almond milk with a pinch of nutmeg), or a spoonful of black sesame paste (黑芝麻糊). Avoid spicy, greasy, or cold foods in the evening — they either stir Yang or tax the Spleen while it should be resting.

4. The Self-Massage Wind-Down (自我按摩)

A 5-minute self-massage sequence amplifies the effects of acupressure and signals the entire body-mind system that the day is complete. Start at the scalp: use your fingertips to gently "comb" through your hair, applying light pressure to the scalp in slow, circular motions. Move to the ears: rub the entire external ear between your thumb and forefinger until it feels warm (the ear is a microcosm of the whole body in TCM auricular therapy). Then press the acupoints in the sequence given earlier: Yin Tang → Anmian → HT7 → SP6 → KI1. This top-to-bottom sequence follows the natural direction of Qi descent for sleep.

"The wise nourish life by following the rhythms of heaven and earth. They sleep when the sun sets and rise when the sun ascends. They do not fight the natural order — and so illness has nowhere to enter."

— Huangdi Neijing (黄帝内经), Chapter on the Four Seasons and Spirit

Frequently Asked Questions

Q: How long does it take for TCM sleep remedies to work?

This depends on the chronicity and root cause of your insomnia. Acute insomnia (days to weeks, often triggered by a specific stressful event) may respond within 3–5 nights of consistent acupressure and herbal tea use. Chronic insomnia (months to years) requires a longer commitment — typically 2–4 weeks of daily practice before significant improvement is noticed. TCM treats the root, not just the symptom, and retraining the body's Yin-Yang rhythm takes time. The key variable is consistency: performing acupressure sporadically or drinking herbal tea only on sleepless nights will produce limited results. Commit to a nightly ritual for 21 days before evaluating its effectiveness.

Q: Can I use these remedies alongside my prescription sleep medication?

In most cases, yes — acupressure, foot soaking, abdominal breathing, and the gentler herbal teas (Chrysanthemum-Goji, Albizia flower) are safe to combine with prescription sleep medications. However, the stronger herbal formulas (particularly Suanzaoren Tang) have genuine sedative effects and may compound the drowsiness caused by medications like zolpidem (Ambien), benzodiazepines, or sedating antidepressants. Always inform your prescribing physician about any herbs you are taking. The goal of TCM treatment, over time, is often to reduce dependence on sleep medications — but this should be done gradually and under medical supervision, never abruptly.

Q: I wake up at 3 AM every night. What does TCM say about this?

Consistent waking at 3 AM falls squarely in the Liver time window (1–3 AM) and the early Lung time window (3–5 AM). In TCM, this pattern is strongly associated with Liver Qi Stagnation — often driven by unprocessed stress, anger, or resentment — combined with some degree of underlying Yin deficiency that prevents you from falling back asleep. The Liver is at peak detoxification between 1–3 AM; if it is congested or inflamed (from alcohol, fatty foods, medications, or emotional stress), the body may wake you during this window. Practical steps: eliminate alcohol completely for two weeks and observe the effect; practice the acupressure sequence focusing on HT7 and SP6; drink Chrysanthemum-Goji tea in the evening; and consider journaling before bed to process any emotional residue from the day. If waking persists, consult a licensed TCM practitioner for a personalized herbal formula — this pattern responds well to formulas that soothe the Liver and nourish Yin, such as modified Suanzaoren Tang or Dan Zhi Xiao Yao San.

Q: Are these remedies safe during pregnancy?

Many of the lifestyle remedies — foot soaking (with warm water only, no added herbs without consultation), abdominal breathing, the "no screen" hour, and gentle scalp massage — are safe and beneficial during pregnancy. However, acupressure requires caution: the point SP6 (Sanyinjiao) is specifically contraindicated during pregnancy because of its strong effect on the uterus. HT7 (Shenmen) and Yin Tang are generally considered safe. Herbal teas require professional guidance — while Chrysanthemum is often used safely during pregnancy in moderation, other herbs like Schisandra and the Suanzaoren Tang formula should only be used under the supervision of a qualified TCM practitioner experienced in prenatal care. Always consult both your obstetrician and a TCM practitioner before starting any herbal regimen during pregnancy.

Q: How does TCM explain why some people are "night owls" and others "morning larks"?

In TCM, your natural sleep-wake tendency reflects your underlying constitution (体质). "Night owls" — people who feel most alert and creative late at night — often have a constitutional Yin deficiency pattern: their cooling, anchoring Yin energy is insufficient to contain their Yang, keeping them wired when they should be winding down. Over time, this pattern can deplete Kidney Yin and lead to more serious sleep disturbances. "Morning larks" — people who wake naturally at dawn — are, from a TCM perspective, in better alignment with the natural rhythm of Yang's daily ascent. This doesn't mean night owls are "unhealthy" — but it does suggest that if you're a night owl who also struggles with insomnia, addressing underlying Yin deficiency through diet (more dark leafy greens, black sesame, goji berries, adequate hydration), lifestyle (earlier wind-down, less screen stimulation), and potentially herbal support (Schisandra, Rehmannia) may help shift your rhythm earlier and improve sleep quality.

Q: Can children use TCM sleep remedies?

Yes — TCM pediatrics has a long tradition of treating childhood sleep disorders, which in Chinese medicine are most often attributed to Spleen Qi deficiency with accumulation of food stagnation, or to Heart Fire disturbing the Shen. Gentle acupressure (HT7 and Yin Tang only, with light pressure) and a consistent bedtime routine with foot soaking are safe for children over age 3. Herbal medicine for children should ALWAYS be prescribed by a licensed TCM pediatric specialist — children's dosages are significantly lower, and their developing systems metabolize herbs differently. A safe dietary intervention for a restless child: a very small serving of warm congee (rice porridge) with a pinch of cinnamon before bed — it gently tonifies the Spleen and anchors the spirit without overstimulating digestion.
