TCM Qi and Blood Theory (气血学说): The Vital Substances That Sustain Life

📅 July 18, 2026 • TCM Fundamentals
Advertisement Space (728x90)

In the vast philosophical and medical landscape of Traditional Chinese Medicine (中医学, Zhōng Yī Xué), no concept is more central — or more practical — than Qi and Blood (气血, Qì Xuè). These two vital substances are the foundation upon which all physiological function rests. When Qi and Blood are abundant and flowing freely, the body is strong, the mind is clear, and disease has no foothold. When they become deficient, stagnant, or imbalanced, the entire system begins to falter — fatigue sets in, immunity drops, sleep deteriorates, and chronic illness takes root.

Understanding Qi and Blood is not merely an academic exercise for TCM students. It is a lens through which anyone can interpret the signals their body sends every day — the afternoon energy crash, the pale complexion in winter, the racing heart after a stressful meeting, the cold hands and feet that never seem to warm up. In this comprehensive guide, we explore the theory, the signs of imbalance, and the practical acupressure, dietary, and lifestyle strategies you can use to nourish your Qi and Blood starting today.

🔑 Key Takeaways

  • Qi (气) is the body's vital energy that drives all physiological functions; Blood (血) is the dense, material nourisher that moistens and sustains tissues
  • Qi and Blood are mutually dependent — Qi moves Blood, and Blood anchors Qi; deficiency in one inevitably affects the other
  • Common signs of Qi Deficiency (气虚) include fatigue, shortness of breath, weak voice, and frequent colds
  • Common signs of Blood Deficiency (血虚) include pale face and lips, dizziness, dry skin, brittle nails, and insomnia
  • The Spleen (脾) is the root of Qi and Blood production — protecting Spleen function through warm, nourishing foods is the single most important dietary strategy
  • Key acupoints like Zusanli (ST36), Sanyinjiao (SP6), and Geshu (BL17) can be stimulated daily to tonify Qi and nourish Blood

What Is Qi (气)?

Qi is one of the most fundamental concepts in Chinese philosophy and medicine, yet it defies simple translation. Often rendered as "vital energy," "life force," or simply "breath," Qi encompasses the dynamic, functional aspect of all life processes. It is the force that warms your body, digests your food, propels blood through your vessels, defends you against pathogens, and holds your organs in their proper places.

The Chinese character for Qi (气) is telling — it depicts steam rising from cooking rice, evoking the idea of energy generated through transformation. In the body, Qi arises primarily from three sources:

  • Prenatal Qi (先天之气, Xiān Tiān Zhī Qì): Inherited from your parents at conception, stored in the Kidneys. This is your constitutional reserve — it determines your baseline vitality and cannot be replenished, only conserved through wise living.
  • Postnatal Qi (后天之气, Hòu Tiān Zhī Qì): Derived from the food you eat and the air you breathe. The Spleen and Stomach extract Gu Qi (谷气, Grain Qi) from food, while the Lungs extract Qing Qi (清气, Clear Qi) from air. These two forms merge in the chest to form Zong Qi (宗气, Gathering Qi).
  • Original Qi (元气, Yuán Qì): A combination of prenatal Qi and postnatal Qi that flows through the San Jiao (三焦, Triple Burner) to warm and motivate all organ functions. It is the body's fundamental driving force.

Qi performs six essential functions in the body: warming (maintaining body temperature), transforming (converting food into usable nutrients), transporting (moving substances to where they are needed), raising (holding organs in place and lifting energy), protecting (defending against external pathogens), and containing (keeping Blood within the vessels and fluids within their proper pathways).

🌿 The Four Types of Qi in Daily Life

Yuan Qi (元气) — your constitutional energy reserves, conserved through adequate sleep and avoiding overexertion. Zong Qi (宗气) — gathering in the chest, supporting heart and lung function; nourished by deep breathing and fresh air. Ying Qi (营气) — nutritive Qi that circulates within the blood vessels, nourishing organs; supported by regular, balanced meals. Wei Qi (卫气) — defensive Qi that patrols the skin surface, protecting against colds and infections; strengthened by moderate exercise and avoiding excessive sweating.

What Is Blood (血, Xuè)?

TCM's concept of Blood (血, Xuè) overlaps with, but is broader than, the Western biomedical understanding. In TCM, Blood is a dense, material substance derived from the transformation of food essence (精华, Jīng Huá) by the Spleen and Stomach, combined with the fluid essence of the Kidneys and propelled by Heart Qi. It circulates through the vessels, nourishing and moistening every tissue, organ, and orifice in the body.

Beyond its physical nourishing role, Blood has a profound relationship with the mind and spirit. The Huangdi Neijing states that "the Blood is the residence of the Shen (神, spirit)." This means that adequate Blood is essential for mental clarity, emotional stability, restful sleep, and a sense of inner peace. When Blood is deficient, the Shen becomes "homeless" — leading to anxiety, insomnia, racing thoughts, and a feeling of being ungrounded.

The organs most closely related to Blood are:

  • The Heart (心): Governs Blood and blood vessels. Heart Qi propels Blood through the circulation; the health of the Heart determines the quality of the pulse and the complexion of the face.
  • The Liver (肝): Stores Blood (藏血, Cáng Xuè) and regulates the volume of Blood circulating at any given time. During rest, Blood returns to the Liver for replenishment; during activity, the Liver releases Blood to the muscles and tendons. The Liver also ensures the smooth flow of Qi, which is essential for Blood circulation.
  • The Spleen (脾): The primary source of Blood production. The Spleen transforms food into Gu Qi and Ying Qi, which are the raw materials for Blood formation. A weak Spleen inevitably leads to Blood Deficiency.
  • The Kidneys (肾): Store Jing (精, Essence), which provides the foundational substance for Marrow (髓, Suǐ) — and Marrow, in TCM, is the origin of Bone Marrow where Blood is produced. Kidney Essence is therefore the deep root of Blood production.
"Qi is the commander of Blood. When Qi moves, Blood follows. Blood is the mother of Qi. Where Blood resides, Qi is nourished." — Classical TCM principle, from the Yi Zong Jin Jian (医宗金鉴, Golden Mirror of Medicine), Qing Dynasty

The Qi-Blood Relationship: A Partnership for Life

The relationship between Qi and Blood is one of the most elegant partnerships in TCM theory. They are inseparable and mutually sustaining:

  • Qi moves Blood (气为血之帅): Qi is the commander that propels Blood through the vessels. Without sufficient Qi, Blood stagnates — leading to cold extremities, fixed pain, blood clots, and menstrual stagnation. This is why Qi Deficiency often manifests alongside Blood Stasis (血瘀).
  • Blood nourishes Qi (血为气之母): Blood is the material foundation that houses and anchors Qi. Without sufficient Blood, Qi becomes scattered and restless — leading to anxiety, palpitations, restlessness, and the sensation of floating energy. After significant blood loss, a person often feels profoundly weak and fatigued — this is Qi collapsing because its "home" (Blood) has been depleted.
  • Qi holds Blood in the vessels: One specific function of Qi — the containing (固摄, Gù Shè) function — is to keep Blood flowing within its proper pathways. When Qi is too weak to contain Blood, symptoms like easy bruising, excessive menstrual bleeding, nosebleeds, or blood in stool or urine can occur.
  • Blood moistens and anchors the organs: While Qi provides the dynamic energy for function, Blood provides the material substrate for structure. Dry skin, brittle nails, thinning hair, blurred vision, and stiff joints are all signs that Blood has become too depleted to properly moisten and nourish the tissues.

This mutual dependency means that in clinical practice, Qi Deficiency and Blood Deficiency frequently appear together. A person who has been chronically fatigued (Qi Deficiency) for months will often develop pale complexion, dizziness, and insomnia (Blood Deficiency) as well — because the weakened Spleen can no longer produce adequate Blood from food, and the weakened Qi can no longer properly move and distribute the Blood that exists.

Signs of Qi Deficiency (气虚, Qì Xū)

Qi Deficiency is arguably the most common pattern seen in modern life. The relentless pace of work, irregular eating habits, overthinking, and chronic stress all conspire to deplete Qi — especially Spleen Qi and Lung Qi. Here are the hallmark signs:

⚠️ Recognizing Qi Deficiency

  • Chronic fatigue: Feeling tired even after adequate rest; the fatigue worsens after eating (postprandial drowsiness) or after physical exertion
  • Shortness of breath: Easily winded, especially with mild exertion like climbing stairs; tendency to sigh frequently
  • Weak, soft voice: Speaking quietly, difficulty projecting the voice; losing voice easily
  • Spontaneous sweating: Breaking into a sweat without exertion, especially on the forehead and upper body; night sweats in severe cases
  • Frequent colds: Wei Qi (defensive Qi) is weakened, leaving the body vulnerable to seasonal pathogens
  • Digestive weakness: Bloating after meals, loose stools, poor appetite, prolapse conditions (hemorrhoids, uterine prolapse)
  • Loose flesh and weak limbs: Muscles lack tone; limbs feel heavy and weak
  • Pale tongue with tooth marks: A classic TCM tongue sign — the tongue is pale, swollen, and shows indentations along the edges where it presses against the teeth

The organs most commonly affected by Qi Deficiency are the Spleen (digestive weakness, fatigue, bloating), the Lung (shortness of breath, frequent colds, spontaneous sweating), and the Kidney (cold extremities, low back weakness, frequent urination, reduced libido). In severe cases, Qi Deficiency can lead to Qi Sinking (气陷) — where the raising function of Qi fails, causing organ prolapse, chronic diarrhea, or a sensation of bearing down.

Signs of Blood Deficiency (血虚, Xuè Xū)

Blood Deficiency is especially common among women (due to menstrual blood loss), vegetarians and vegans (if diet is not carefully planned), the elderly, and anyone recovering from illness or surgery. Because Blood nourishes the entire body and houses the Shen, its deficiency produces a wide range of symptoms:

⚠️ Recognizing Blood Deficiency

  • Pale complexion, lips, and nail beds: The most visible external sign — the face loses its rosy glow and appears sallow or washed out
  • Dizziness and lightheadedness: Especially upon standing quickly; sometimes accompanied by visual disturbances like floaters or blurred vision
  • Dry skin, hair, and nails: Skin becomes rough and flaky; hair loses luster and may fall out; nails become brittle and develop vertical ridges
  • Insomnia and restless sleep: Difficulty falling asleep, vivid dreams, frequent waking — the Shen has no Blood "home" to rest in
  • Palpitations and anxiety: A sensation of the heart racing or fluttering; feeling unsettled and anxious without clear cause
  • Scanty or absent menstruation: Light, pale menstrual flow; amenorrhea (absent periods); long menstrual cycles
  • Numbness in the limbs: Tingling or numbness in the hands, feet, or extremities — the Blood is insufficient to nourish the sinews and skin
  • Pale tongue and thin pulse: The tongue body is pale, thin, and may have a slightly dry surface; the pulse feels thin (细, Xì) and weak under the fingers

Blood Stasis (血瘀, Xuè Yū): When Blood Stops Moving

While Qi Deficiency and Blood Deficiency are conditions of insufficient substance, Blood Stasis (血瘀) is a condition of stagnation — Blood that has stopped flowing properly and accumulates in areas of the body. Blood Stasis can arise from Qi Deficiency (not enough Qi to move Blood), Qi Stagnation (Liver Qi constraint from emotional stress), Cold (which congeals and slows Blood), Heat (which evaporates the fluid component of Blood, making it thick), trauma, or chronic illness.

The hallmark of Blood Stasis is fixed, stabbing pain that is worse at night. Other signs include dark complexion, dark spots on the face (melasma), purple lips or nails, varicose veins, fixed masses or lumps, and in women, dark menstrual blood with clots and severe menstrual pain that improves when clots are passed. The tongue in Blood Stasis typically shows purple spots or a purple body, and the pulse may feel choppy (涩, Sè).

⚠️ When to Seek Professional Help

While mild Qi and Blood imbalances can be addressed through diet and lifestyle, certain symptoms warrant professional TCM consultation or medical evaluation: persistent fatigue lasting more than two weeks, unexplained weight loss, severe menstrual irregularities, chronic pain, blood in stool or urine, rapid heartbeat at rest, or fainting episodes. TCM practitioners use herbal formulas like Si Wu Tang (四物汤) for Blood Deficiency and Bu Zhong Yi Qi Tang (补中益气汤) for Qi Deficiency — these require professional diagnosis and dosing.

Acupressure Points to Tonify Qi and Nourish Blood

Acupressure offers an accessible, daily method to support Qi and Blood production. The following five acupoints are among the most powerful and widely used in TCM for this purpose. Stimulate each point with firm, circular pressure for 1–2 minutes, once or twice daily.

🔴 Zusanli — ST36 (足三里, Zú Sān Lǐ)

Location: Four finger-widths below the kneecap, one finger-width lateral to the shin bone (tibia). You'll feel a tender depression when you press correctly.

Action: The single most important point for tonifying Spleen and Stomach Qi. Known as the "point of longevity" (长寿穴, Cháng Shòu Xué), ST36 strengthens digestion, boosts immunity, increases energy, and promotes the production of Qi and Blood from food. The Huangdi Neijing states that moxibustion on ST36 can treat a hundred diseases.

How to use: Press firmly with your thumb in a clockwise circular motion for 1–2 minutes on each leg, morning and evening. For stronger effect, use moxibustion (moxa stick) held 1–2 inches above the point for 10 minutes.

🟡 Sanyinjiao — SP6 (三阴交, Sān Yīn Jiāo)

Location: Four finger-widths above the inner ankle bone, on the posterior border of the tibia (shin bone).

Action: The meeting point of three Yin meridians — Spleen, Liver, and Kidney. This makes it the premier point for nourishing Blood, regulating menstruation, calming the mind, and strengthening the Spleen's transformative function. It is indispensable for women's health issues but benefits everyone with Blood Deficiency.

How to use: Press with moderate force using your thumb, rotating clockwise for 1–2 minutes per leg. Avoid during pregnancy (this point can stimulate uterine contractions).

🟠 Geshu — BL17 (膈俞, Gé Shū)

Location: On the back, 1.5 cun lateral to the lower border of the spinous process of the seventh thoracic vertebra (about level with the bottom of the shoulder blades, slightly to either side of the spine).

Action: Known as the "Influential Point of Blood" (血会, Xuè Huì), BL17 is the single most important back-shu point for treating all Blood-related conditions — Blood Deficiency, Blood Stasis, and Heat in the Blood. It nourishes Blood, invigorates circulation, and cools Blood Heat.

How to use: Use a tennis ball placed against a wall and roll your upper back over it, targeting the area between the shoulder blades. Alternatively, ask a partner to press this point with their thumbs for 2 minutes. Moxibustion here is especially effective for Blood Deficiency.

🔴 Qihai — CV6 (气海, Qì Hǎi)

Location: On the midline of the lower abdomen, 1.5 cun (approximately two finger-widths) below the navel.

Action: Literally "Sea of Qi," this point is the primary source point for tonifying the body's entire Qi reserve. It strengthens Spleen Qi, warms Kidney Yang, and promotes the overall vitality of the body's energy system. It is especially valuable for people with chronic fatigue, weak digestion, and cold lower abdomen.

How to use: Place your palm over the point and apply gentle, warming pressure in a clockwise circular motion for 2–3 minutes. For best results, warm your hands by rubbing them together first. Moxibustion on CV6 is a classical tonic therapy in TCM, traditionally performed in winter to build reserves for the coming year.

🟢 Xuehai — SP10 (血海, Xuè Hǎi)

Location: On the inner thigh, approximately two finger-widths above the superior medial corner of the patella (kneecap), in the bulge of the vastus medialis muscle when the knee is flexed.

Action: Literally "Sea of Blood," SP10 is the primary point for nourishing and invigorating Blood. It treats Blood Deficiency (pale complexion, dizziness), Blood Stasis (painful menstruation, dark clots), and Blood Heat (skin rashes, eczema, acne). It is a must-know point for anyone interested in women's health or skin conditions.

How to use: Press firmly with your thumb in a clockwise circular motion for 1–2 minutes on each leg. Combine with SP6 for a powerful Blood-nourishing protocol.

Dietary Strategies to Nourish Qi and Blood

In TCM, food is the first medicine. The Spleen and Stomach are responsible for extracting Qi and Blood from the food we eat, so protecting and strengthening digestive function through dietary choices is the most natural way to replenish Qi and Blood. Here are the core principles:

Foods That Tonify Qi

  • Rice (大米, Dà Mǐ): The foundational Spleen-strengthening grain in Chinese dietary therapy. Warm rice congee (粥) is the ideal Qi-building breakfast — easy to digest, gently warming, and deeply nourishing to the middle Jiao.
  • Sweet potato and pumpkin: Naturally sweet, warm foods that tonify Spleen Qi. Bake, steam, or add to congee.
  • Chicken (鸡肉): Warm in nature, chicken tonifies Qi and Blood simultaneously. The classic Huang Qi Ji Tang (黄芪鸡汤 — astragalus chicken soup) is a premier Qi-building dish, especially during autumn and winter.
  • Dates and longan: Jujube dates (红枣) and longan fruit (龙眼) tonify both Qi and Blood, strengthen the Spleen, and calm the spirit. Eat as snacks or brew as tea.
  • Astragalus root (黄芪): The supreme Qi-tonifying herb. Add 10–15g of sliced astragalus to soups, congee, or brew as a daily tea. It strengthens Wei Qi (immunity) and raises Spleen Qi to prevent prolapse.

Foods That Nourish Blood

  • Black sesame seeds (黑芝麻): Nourish Liver and Kidney Blood, darken hair, moisten dryness. Add ground sesame to porridge, oatmeal, or smoothies.
  • Beef and lamb: Red meats are among the most direct Blood-building foods in TCM. Slow-cooked beef or lamb stew with warming spices like ginger and cinnamon provides deep nourishment.
  • Spinach and dark leafy greens: Rich in iron and folate, greens nourish Liver Blood. Lightly cooked (not raw) to protect Spleen Yang.
  • Dang Gui (当归, Angelica sinensis): The premier Blood-nourishing herb in TCM. Use 3–5g in slow-cooked soups with chicken or pork. The classic Dang Gui Bu Xue Tang (当归补血汤) combines dang gui with astragalus for powerful Qi-Blood nourishment.
  • Goji berries (枸杞): Nourish Liver Blood and Kidney Yin. Add 10–15 dried berries to teas, soups, or congee daily.
  • Eggs: Particularly egg yolk, which in TCM nourishes Heart Blood and calms the Shen. Soft-boiled eggs are easiest to digest.
  • Beetroot and dark berries: While less prominent in classical TCM, these foods are now recognized in modern Chinese dietary therapy for their Blood-building properties due to high iron and antioxidant content.

🍲 Qi and Blood Nourishing Congee Recipe (气血双补粥)

  • ½ cup white rice (大米)
  • 10g astragalus root slices (黄芪), wrapped in cheesecloth for easy removal
  • 5 jujube dates, pitted (红枣)
  • 10g goji berries (枸杞)
  • 10g longan flesh (龙眼肉)
  • 5g dang gui slices (当归) — optional, for women post-menstruation

Combine rice with 6 cups of water and the astragalus bundle in a pot. Bring to a boil, then simmer on low heat for 45–60 minutes, stirring occasionally, until the rice breaks down into a thick porridge. Add the dates, longan, and dang gui in the last 20 minutes. Add goji berries in the last 5 minutes. Remove the astragalus bundle. Eat warm for breakfast. This gentle congee simultaneously tonifies Spleen Qi, nourishes Blood, and calms the spirit — an ideal daily tonic for anyone recovering from fatigue, illness, or menstrual depletion.

Lifestyle Practices for Qi and Blood Health

Beyond acupressure and diet, daily habits play a crucial role in maintaining Qi and Blood balance:

  • Protect your Spleen with warm food: The Spleen is easily damaged by cold, raw foods, and excessive sugar. Favor warm, cooked meals. Avoid ice-cold drinks, smoothies, and large salads — especially in the morning when the digestive fire is just waking up.
  • Eat at regular times: The Stomach has its peak energy window between 7–9 AM (Stomach meridian time) and the Spleen between 9–11 AM. Eating a substantial, warm breakfast during these hours maximizes Qi and Blood production from food.
  • Move gently every day: Moderate exercise like walking, Tai Chi, or Baduanjin Qigong promotes Qi circulation and Blood flow without depleting reserves. Overtraining and extreme endurance exercise, however, can damage Qi and consume Blood — the TCM equivalent of "burning the candle at both ends."
  • Sleep before 11 PM: The Liver stores Blood during rest. Going to bed by 10:30–11 PM allows the Liver to properly replenish its Blood reserves during the deep sleep hours of 1–3 AM (Liver meridian time). Chronic late nights directly deplete Liver Blood.
  • Manage emotional stress: Worry and overthinking damage Spleen Qi (思伤脾). Anger and resentment constrain Liver Qi, leading to Blood Stasis. Fear depletes Kidney Qi, undermining the deep root of Blood production. Regular meditation, breathing exercises, and time in nature help regulate emotions and protect Qi-Blood balance.
  • Menstrual care for women: During menstruation, avoid cold foods, cold water swimming, and excessive physical exertion. The classical TCM advice is to "warm the Blood and nourish during the post-menstrual period" — the week after menstruation is the optimal time to eat Blood-building foods like dang gui chicken soup, red date tea, and black sesame porridge.

✨ Benefits of Nourishing Qi and Blood

  • Sustained energy throughout the day without caffeine crashes
  • Clear, rosy complexion and healthy, lustrous hair
  • Deep, restorative sleep and calm emotional state
  • Strong immunity — fewer colds and faster recovery from illness
  • Healthy, regular menstruation with less pain and adequate flow
  • Sharp mental focus and stable concentration
  • Warm extremities and comfortable body temperature regulation
  • Resilient digestion that extracts maximum nourishment from food

Qi and Blood Through the Seasons

TCM teaches that our Qi and Blood needs shift with the seasons. Aligning your nourishing practices with nature's rhythms amplifies their effectiveness:

  • Spring (春): The Liver is most active. Focus on moving Liver Qi (gentle stretching, green vegetables) to ensure smooth Blood circulation. Avoid excessive sour foods, which can over-constrain the Liver.
  • Summer (夏): Heat scatters Qi through sweating. Protect Qi by avoiding excessive sun exposure and staying hydrated with room-temperature or warm water. Nourish Heart Blood with lotus seeds, longan, and lily bulb.
  • Autumn (秋): Dryness depletes Blood's moistening function. Eat moistening foods like pear, white fungus (银耳), lily bulb, and honey. This is the season to begin building Blood reserves for winter.
  • Winter (冬): The Kidney stores Essence — the deep root of Blood. Eat warming, nourishing foods like bone broth, lamb, black sesame, and walnuts. Rest more, sleep earlier, and use moxibustion on ST36 and CV6 to build Qi reserves for the coming spring.
"Qi is the commander of Blood. Blood is the mother of Qi. When Qi moves, Blood follows. When Qi stagnates, Blood congeals. When Blood is deficient, Qi scatters. To heal the body, one must understand this dance — and restore its harmony." — Principle of TCM physiology, as taught in the Zhong Yi Ji Chu Li Lun (中医基础理论, Basic Theory of Traditional Chinese Medicine)

Putting It All Together: Your Daily Qi-Blood Routine

Here is a simple, practical daily routine that integrates the acupressure, dietary, and lifestyle strategies discussed in this article:

  • Morning (7–9 AM): Wake before 7 AM. Drink a cup of warm water. Eat a warm Spleen-friendly breakfast — congee with dates and goji berries, or warm oatmeal with black sesame and longan. Press ST36 and SP6 for 1 minute each while waiting for breakfast.
  • Midday (11 AM–1 PM): Heart meridian time. Eat lunch mindfully, avoiding screens. A bowl of rice with cooked vegetables and protein supports afternoon Qi. Press CV6 gently after eating to support digestion.
  • Afternoon (3–5 PM): Kidney meridian time. This is the natural low point of energy — resist the urge for coffee. Instead, brew a cup of goji chrysanthemum tea or astragalus tea. Take a 10-minute walk to circulate Qi.
  • Evening (7–9 PM): Pericardium meridian time — ideal for gentle self-care. Press SP10 (Sea of Blood) on both legs. Eat a light dinner — avoid heavy, greasy foods that burden the Spleen at night.
  • Bedtime (9:30–10:30 PM): Wind down with lotus seed and lily bulb sweet soup or longan red date tea. Press BL17 using a tennis ball against the wall. Be in bed by 10:30 PM to support the Liver's Blood storage function during the 1–3 AM window.

Nourishing Qi and Blood is not about dramatic overnight change — it is the quiet, patient work of daily choices: warm food over cold, regular meals over skipped breakfasts, gentle movement over exhaustion, early rest over late-night scrolling. Over weeks and months, these small acts of self-care accumulate into profound vitality. As the ancient Chinese saying goes: "滴水穿石" (dī shuǐ chuān shí) — dripping water penetrates stone. Let the slow, steady nourishment of Qi and Blood be your path to lasting health.
