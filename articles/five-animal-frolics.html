Five Animal Frolics

📅 June 16, 2026 • Daoyin Exercise
Advertisement Space (728x90)

Chinese Name: 五禽戏 (Wǔ Qín Xì)

Meaning: "Five Animal Frolics"

The Five Animal Frolics is one of the oldest and most celebrated Daoyin exercise sets. Created by the legendary physician Hua Tuo (华佗) during the Eastern Han Dynasty (25-220 CE), this system mimics the natural movements of five animals: tiger, deer, bear, monkey, and crane. Each animal's movements target specific organ systems in Traditional Chinese Medicine.

The Five Animals

🐯 Tiger (虎) — Liver & Gallbladder

Tiger movements are powerful and dynamic, involving clawing, pouncing, and twisting gestures. The tiger builds courage and strengthens the muscles and tendons. Tiger practice is associated with the Wood element and helps release frustration and anger. Stand with feet wide, hands form "claws," and pounce forward with focused intensity.

🦌 Deer (鹿) — Kidneys

Deer movements are graceful and agile, focusing on the lower back and kidneys. The movements include antler gestures, gentle twisting, and playful bounding. Deer practice improves flexibility, strengthens the lower back, and supports reproductive health. Move with the lightness and curiosity of a deer in the forest.

🐻 Bear (熊) — Spleen & Stomach

Bear movements are slow, heavy, and grounded, targeting the digestive system. The movements include swaying, stomping, and leaning from side to side. Bear practice improves digestion, builds stability, and cultivates inner strength. Stand with feet wide and move with the deliberate power of a bear.

🐒 Monkey (猴) — Heart

Monkey movements are quick, playful, and alert, targeting the heart system. The movements include scratching, jumping, looking around, and picking fruit. Monkey practice improves alertness, enhances coordination, and brings joy and playfulness to the practice. Move with the mischievous energy of a monkey.

🦢 Crane (鹤) — Lungs

Crane movements are elegant and balanced, focusing on the lungs and breathing. The movements include wing spreading, one-legged standing, and slow, graceful turns. Crane practice improves breathing, enhances balance, and promotes longevity. Stand tall and move with the dignity of a crane.

How to Practice

  • Practice 15-25 minutes, ideally in the morning
  • You can practice all five animals in sequence or focus on one per day
  • Each animal sequence includes 2-4 specific movements
  • Coordinate movements with natural breathing
  • Try to embody the spirit of each animal—feel their energy, not just their movements

Health Benefits

  • Strengthens organ systems: Each animal targets specific TCM organs
  • Improves agility and coordination: The variety of movements trains different movement patterns
  • Boosts immune function: Organ stimulation supports overall health
  • Increases flexibility: The twisting and bending movements maintain joint mobility
  • Emotional balance: Each animal helps process specific emotions (anger, fear, worry, joy, grief)
"To move like the animals is to remember our connection to nature. In their movements, we find the medicine our bodies need." — Attributed to Hua Tuo (华佗)
