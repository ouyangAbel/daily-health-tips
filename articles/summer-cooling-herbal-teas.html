Summer Cooling Herbal Teas Guide: 8 TCM Recipes to Beat the Heat Naturally

📅 June 30, 2026 • Nutrition
Advertisement Space (728x90)

When the summer sun beats down and the air feels thick with humidity, nothing restores balance like a cup of cooling herbal tea. For thousands of years, Traditional Chinese Medicine (TCM) has prescribed herbal infusions — known as 凉茶 (liáng chá) — not merely as refreshing beverages but as gentle, daily medicine. Unlike iced drinks that shock the digestive system, cooling herbal teas work with your body to clear internal heat, replenish lost fluids, and restore harmony between Yin and Yang.

In this comprehensive guide, you will discover eight time-honored TCM cooling tea recipes, each with its unique properties, ideal brewing method, and guidance on who benefits most from drinking it. Whether you are struggling with summer fatigue, skin breakouts, irritability, or simply the oppressive heat, there is a tea here for you. These are not just drinks — they are a practical expression of the TCM principle that true cooling comes from within.

Key Takeaways

  • Cooling herbal teas work by clearing excess heat from specific organs — the Lungs, Liver, Heart, and Stomach — according to TCM theory
  • Eight classic teas each target different heat patterns: chrysanthemum for eye heat, honeysuckle for skin inflammation, mint for upper body, and more
  • Herbal teas should be consumed warm or at room temperature — never ice-cold — to protect the Spleen and Stomach's digestive function
  • Timing matters: drink cooling teas in the morning and early afternoon; avoid them late at night or on an empty stomach
  • Not all cooling teas suit everyone — those with weak digestion, cold constitution, or during menstruation should choose milder options

Why Herbal Teas Are Essential for Summer Health

In TCM, summer belongs to the Fire element and is governed by the Heart meridian. When external heat accumulates in the body faster than it can be released, the result is what TCM calls "Summer Heat" (暑热, Shǔ Rè) — a pattern of excessive Yang that manifests as thirst, sweating, irritability, poor appetite, skin rashes, and fatigue. Unlike Western medicine's view of heat as a simple temperature regulation problem, TCM sees summer heat as an energetic imbalance that affects multiple organ systems simultaneously.

Cooling herbal teas address this imbalance through the TCM concept of "clearing heat" (清热, Qīng Rè). Each herb in these teas carries a thermal nature — cool or cold — and an affinity toward specific meridians. By drinking the right tea, you are essentially delivering targeted cooling energy to the organs that need it most: chrysanthemum cools the Liver and eyes, honeysuckle clears the Lungs and skin, bamboo leaf drains Heart fire. This is why herbal teas feel different from simply drinking cold water — they work with your body's energy system.

Moreover, summer heat tends to combine with Dampness (湿, Shī), especially in humid climates. When heat and dampness mix, you feel heavy, bloated, and mentally foggy — a condition many teas in this guide are also designed to resolve. Lotus leaf, mung bean, and bamboo leaf are particularly effective at clearing both heat and dampness, making them indispensable during muggy summer days.

"A cup of herbal tea in summer is worth more than a thousand gold coins — it cools the fire within, moistens what is dry, and restores what the sun has taken."

— Traditional Chinese Medicine proverb

8 Cooling Herbal Tea Recipes for Summer

Below are eight classic TCM herbal teas, arranged from the most gentle and universally suitable to the most potent. Each entry includes the tea's Chinese name with pinyin, its TCM thermal nature and meridian affinities, a simple brewing method, and guidance on who will benefit most. Unless otherwise noted, all teas can be consumed 1–3 times per day during the hottest months.

1. Chrysanthemum Tea (菊花茶, Júhuā Chá) — The Gentle Eye Soother

TCM Properties: Slightly cold in nature; enters the Lung and Liver meridians. Clears heat from the head and eyes, calms the Liver, and brightens vision.

Brewing Method: Add 8–12 dried chrysanthemum flowers (white or yellow varieties both work) to a glass teapot. Pour hot water — not quite boiling, around 90°C / 194°F — over the flowers and steep for 5–8 minutes. The flowers will slowly open and release their delicate fragrance. Add 2–3 goji berries for extra eye nourishment and a touch of natural sweetness. Drink 1–2 cups daily, especially in the late morning.

Best For: People who spend long hours in front of screens experiencing eye strain, dryness, or redness; those prone to summer headaches or irritability; anyone seeking a mild, daily cooling tea with minimal side effects. This tea is gentle enough for most constitutions and can be enjoyed throughout the summer.

2. Honeysuckle Tea (金银花茶, Jīnyínhuā Chá) — The Skin-Clearing Guardian

TCM Properties: Cold in nature; enters the Lung, Heart, and Stomach meridians. Clears heat and toxins, disperses Wind-Heat, and cools the blood.

Brewing Method: Use 5–8 grams of dried honeysuckle flowers. Rinse briefly in cool water, then add to a pot. Pour 500ml of boiling water over the flowers and steep, covered, for 10–15 minutes. The tea will have a pale golden color and a subtly sweet, floral aroma. Strain and drink warm. For extra detoxifying power, add a few slices of fresh ginger to balance the cold nature. Drink 1 cup daily for no more than 5 consecutive days.

Best For: Those experiencing summer skin breakouts, heat rashes, or acne flare-ups; early-stage colds with sore throat and fever; people in hot, polluted urban environments. Honeysuckle is especially valued during summer for its ability to prevent and treat heat-related skin conditions.

3. Mint Tea (薄荷茶, Bòhé Chá) — The Instant Upper Body Cooler

TCM Properties: Cool in nature; enters the Lung and Liver meridians. Disperses Wind-Heat, clears the head and eyes, promotes the flow of Liver Qi, and relieves throat discomfort.

Brewing Method: Use 3–5 grams of fresh mint leaves (or 2 grams dried). Place the leaves in a cup and pour hot water — about 85°C / 185°F, as boiling water can destroy the delicate essential oils. Cover immediately and steep for only 3–5 minutes. The volatile oils that give mint its cooling sensation dissipate quickly, so drink it fresh and avoid over-steeping. A slice of lemon or a teaspoon of honey enhances both flavor and cooling effect.

Best For: Quick relief from summer headaches, dizziness, or a sensation of heat rising to the head; people feeling irritable or emotionally "heated"; those experiencing throat discomfort or a stuffy sensation in the chest. Mint tea provides the fastest cooling sensation of all teas in this guide, making it ideal for immediate relief.

4. Lotus Leaf Tea (荷叶茶, Héyè Chá) — The Dampness-Draining Slimmer

TCM Properties: Neutral to slightly cool in nature; enters the Liver, Spleen, and Stomach meridians. Clears summer heat, raises clear Yang, stops bleeding, and most importantly, transforms dampness and promotes urination.

Brewing Method: Break 5–10 grams of dried lotus leaf into small pieces. Add to a pot with 600ml of water. Bring to a boil, then reduce heat and simmer gently for 10–15 minutes. Strain and drink warm. The tea has a mild, grassy, slightly bitter taste — you can add a single red date (红枣) during simmering to mellow the flavor. Drink 1–2 cups daily, ideally between meals.

Best For: Those suffering from summer dampness — feeling heavy, bloated, or water-retained; people aiming to support healthy weight management during summer; anyone experiencing a foggy, heavy-headed feeling in humid weather. Lotus leaf is one of TCM's most respected herbs for draining dampness without depleting the body's energy.

5. Bamboo Leaf Tea (竹叶茶, Zhúyè Chá) — The Heart Fire Extinguisher

TCM Properties: Cold in nature; enters the Heart, Stomach, and Small Intestine meridians. Clears Heart fire, relieves irritability, promotes urination, and drains heat through the urinary tract.

Brewing Method: Use 6–10 grams of fresh or dried light bamboo leaves (淡竹叶, Dàn Zhú Yè). Rinse quickly, then add to a pot with 500ml of water. Bring to a boil and simmer for 10 minutes. The tea is naturally light and slightly sweet. Combine with 2–3 slices of licorice root (甘草) for a classic TCM pairing that enhances both cooling effect and flavor. Drink 1 cup in the afternoon when heat and irritability peak.

Best For: People experiencing summer irritability, restlessness, or difficulty sleeping due to heat; those with mouth ulcers or a bitter taste in the mouth (classic signs of Heart fire in TCM); anyone prone to urinary discomfort during hot weather. Bamboo leaf tea specifically targets the Heart meridian, making it uniquely effective for emotional heat.

6. Mung Bean Tisane (绿豆汤, Lǜdòu Tāng) — The Classic Summer Detoxifier

TCM Properties: Cool in nature; enters the Heart and Stomach meridians. Clears summer heat, detoxifies, reduces inflammation, and quenches thirst more deeply than water alone.

Brewing Method: Rinse 1 cup of dried mung beans. Add to a pot with 8 cups of water and 2 slices of fresh ginger (essential for protecting the Spleen from the beans' cooling nature). Bring to a boil, then reduce heat and simmer for 30–40 minutes until the beans split open and the water turns a soft green. Strain the liquid for a clear tisane, or leave the softened beans in for a more substantial drink. Add rock sugar to taste. Serve warm or at room temperature — never ice-cold.

Best For: The most iconic summer drink in Chinese culture, suitable for most people during peak heat; those experiencing heat rash, excessive thirst, or a sensation of internal burning; anyone needing deep hydration and gentle detoxification. Mung bean tisane can be consumed 2–3 times per week throughout the summer season.

7. Sour Plum Drink (酸梅汤, Suānméi Tāng) — The Yin-Nourishing Thirst Quencher

TCM Properties: Neutral to slightly cool; enters the Liver, Spleen, Lung, and Large Intestine meridians. Generates body fluids, astringes Yin, relieves summer heat, and quenches thirst — all while supporting digestion.

Brewing Method: Combine 8–10 dried smoked plums (乌梅, Wū Méi), 3 slices of licorice root, 2 pieces of dried tangerine peel (陈皮), 1–2 hawthorn berries (山楂), and a small piece of osmanthus flower (桂花) in a pot with 1.5 liters of water. Bring to a boil, then simmer for 30–40 minutes. Strain and add rock sugar while still warm. Chill naturally to room temperature before drinking — the sour-sweet flavor is deeply refreshing.

Best For: People who sweat heavily and feel perpetually thirsty despite drinking water; those experiencing summer fatigue with dry mouth and throat; anyone whose digestion feels sluggish in the heat. Sour plum drink is unique among cooling drinks because it simultaneously generates fluids and conserves Yin — making it ideal for those who are easily dehydrated.

8. Xia Sang Ju (夏桑菊, Xià Sāng Jú) — The Comprehensive Summer Formula

TCM Properties: Cool to cold in nature; combines three herbs targeting multiple meridians. Xia Ku Cao (夏枯草, Prunella) enters the Liver and Gallbladder, clearing heat from the head and eyes; Sang Ye (桑叶, Mulberry Leaf) enters the Lung and Liver, dispersing Wind-Heat; Ju Hua (菊花, Chrysanthemum) enters the Lung and Liver, cooling the eyes and calming the Liver.

Brewing Method: Use 10g Xia Ku Cao, 8g dried mulberry leaves, and 8g chrysanthemum flowers. Add all ingredients to a pot with 1 liter of water. Bring to a boil, then simmer gently for 15–20 minutes. Strain well. This tea has a distinct herbal, slightly bitter taste — add a small amount of rock sugar or honey if needed. Drink 1 cup per day, ideally in the morning or early afternoon. Do not exceed 3 consecutive days of use.

Best For: This is the most potent formula in the guide, reserved for the hottest days of deep summer. It is excellent for those experiencing red, swollen, or painful eyes; severe summer headaches with eye pressure; people with a tendency toward high blood pressure that worsens in summer; those who find single-herb teas insufficient for their heat symptoms. Because of its strength, it is not a daily casual tea — use it strategically during heat waves.

Important Drinking Precautions

While cooling herbal teas are natural and generally safe, they are still medicine in the TCM framework. Using them incorrectly can create new imbalances even as they resolve others. Here are the most important precautions to keep in mind.

Never Drink Ice-Cold

The single most common mistake is serving cooling teas over ice. In TCM, extreme cold shocks the Spleen and Stomach — the organs responsible for transforming food and fluids into usable energy. When you drink ice-cold tea, you temporarily cool your mouth but actually force your body to expend extra energy warming the liquid to body temperature. This weakens the digestive fire over time, leading to bloating, loose stools, and ironically, more internal heat accumulation. Always drink cooling teas at room temperature or gently warm.

Know Your Constitution

People with a naturally cold constitution (阳虚体质, Yang Deficiency) — those who often feel cold, have cold hands and feet, prefer warm drinks, and tend toward loose stools — should be cautious with cooling teas. For such individuals, choose the mildest options (chrysanthemum or lotus leaf) and always add a slice of ginger. People actively experiencing diarrhea, during menstruation, or recovering from illness should pause cooling teas entirely and focus on warming, nourishing foods instead.

Timing and Duration Matter

Cooling teas should be consumed during the active, Yang part of the day — morning and early afternoon. Drinking them in the evening or before bed can interfere with the body's natural transition into Yin (rest) mode. Additionally, even the gentlest cooling teas should not be consumed daily without breaks. Alternate between tea types, take 2–3 days off per week, and reduce or pause consumption when the hottest weather passes or if you notice any digestive discomfort.

Watch for Signs of Overcooling

Your body will tell you if you have exceeded your cooling threshold. Signs include: loose stools or diarrhea, a sensation of cold in the stomach, loss of appetite, increased fatigue rather than refreshment, and a pale or swollen tongue with a white coating. If you notice any of these signs, stop all cooling teas immediately and switch to warm ginger tea or plain warm water for several days to restore digestive warmth.

Daily Herbal Tea Drinking Schedule

Integrating cooling teas into your daily rhythm maximizes their benefits while minimizing any risk. Here is a suggested schedule for a typical summer day, designed around TCM's organ clock and the natural movement of Yang energy.

Morning (7:00 AM – 9:00 AM) — Stomach Time

Start your day with a cup of warm chrysanthemum tea or mild lotus leaf tea after breakfast — never on an empty stomach. The Stomach meridian is most active during these hours, and a gentle cooling tea at this time supports the digestive system's ability to process food without adding internal heat. Pair with a light breakfast of congee or steamed buns for ideal synergy.

Late Morning (10:00 AM – 11:00 AM) — Spleen Time

This is the best window for moderately cooling teas like honeysuckle or bamboo leaf. The Spleen meridian governs fluid metabolism, and a cooling tea during this window helps the body manage hydration efficiently throughout the afternoon. If you are prone to skin breakouts, honeysuckle tea at this time is especially beneficial.

Early Afternoon (1:00 PM – 3:00 PM) — Small Intestine Time

The peak of Yang energy and often the hottest part of the day. This is the ideal time for stronger cooling formulas like Xia Sang Ju or sour plum drink. The body's energy is at its maximum, allowing it to handle more cooling without compromising the digestive fire. Drink your tea slowly over 15–20 minutes while resting in shade.

Late Afternoon (3:00 PM – 5:00 PM) — Bladder Time

A cup of lotus leaf or bamboo leaf tea during this window supports the Bladder meridian's function of eliminating waste via urine — particularly helpful for clearing any dampness or heat that has accumulated during the day. Keep this cup lighter than your earlier servings.

Evening (After 6:00 PM) — Transition to Yin

As the body shifts toward rest mode, stop all cooling teas. Instead, switch to plain warm water or, if desired, a very mild chrysanthemum tea with a single red date added to balance the cooling nature. Avoid all cold-natured teas in the evening to protect the Kidneys and ensure restful sleep.

Weekly Rhythm

Rotate between 2–3 different teas over the course of a week rather than drinking the same one every day. For example: Monday and Thursday — chrysanthemum tea, Tuesday and Friday — mung bean tisane, Wednesday — lotus leaf tea. Take Saturday and Sunday as cooling-tea-free days unless a heat wave demands otherwise. This rotation prevents any single herb from accumulating excessively and keeps your body responsive to each tea's benefits.

Frequently Asked Questions About Cooling Herbal Teas

Can I drink cooling herbal teas every day during summer?

Not every single day without breaks. Most cooling teas are safe 4–5 days per week for people with a balanced constitution, but taking 2–3 days off per week is recommended. This prevents the cooling nature of the herbs from gradually weakening your digestive fire (Spleen Yang). If you feel fine and your digestion remains normal, you can maintain a 5-days-on, 2-days-off rhythm. If you notice any digestive changes, reduce to 3 days per week and always add ginger.

Can I combine multiple cooling teas into one blend?

Generally, it is safer to drink one tea at a time rather than mixing multiple cooling herbs unless you are following a traditional formula like Xia Sang Ju. Combining too many cold-natured herbs can overwhelm the digestive system and create unwanted side effects. If you do want to blend, stick to complementary pairs — chrysanthemum with goji berry, or mint with green tea — and avoid mixing more than 2–3 herbs unless under the guidance of a qualified TCM practitioner.

Are cooling herbal teas safe for children and the elderly?

For children over 5 years old, chrysanthemum tea with a red date is the safest option, served lukewarm and in half the adult quantity. Avoid stronger teas like Xia Sang Ju for children. For elderly individuals, especially those who tend to feel cold, cooling teas should be used sparingly — perhaps once every few days during the hottest weather only. Always add ginger and a red date to moderate the cooling effect when serving to the elderly. Pregnant women should avoid all cooling herbal teas without consulting a TCM practitioner first.

Can I prepare these teas in advance and store them?

Yes, but with limits. Most cooling teas can be brewed in the morning, strained, and kept in a thermos (to maintain warmth) or at room temperature for consumption throughout the day. Do not refrigerate and reheat — the temperature shock damages the tea's energetic properties. Never drink yesterday's tea, as the volatile compounds degrade and the tea can develop unwanted bacterial growth. The exception is sour plum drink, which is traditionally brewed in larger batches and can be kept in the refrigerator for up to 2 days — but bring it to room temperature before drinking.

What if I don't feel any cooling effect from these teas?

Cooling herbal teas work gently and cumulatively — they are not designed to produce a sudden, dramatic cooling sensation like ice water. If you don't notice an immediate effect, be patient and consistent. The cooling manifests as better tolerance of heat over days, reduced irritability, clearer skin, and more restful sleep. If after 5–7 days of consistent use you still feel no benefit, you may need a stronger formula (move from chrysanthemum to Xia Sang Ju) or your heat pattern may require a different approach — consult a TCM practitioner for personalized guidance.

Can I add sweeteners to these teas?

Yes, but choose wisely. Rock sugar (冰糖) is the traditional TCM sweetener for cooling teas because it is neutral in thermal nature and adds sweetness without creating dampness. Honey can be used but should be added when the tea has cooled to drinkable temperature — adding honey to boiling water destroys its beneficial enzymes. Avoid white sugar, which TCM considers to generate dampness, and artificial sweeteners, which have no role in the traditional framework. If you are drinking the tea for its medicinal properties, minimal or no sweetener is best.
