Wise Owl Gazes Behind

📅 June 16, 2026 • Baduanjin Movement 4
Advertisement Space (728x90)

Chinese Name: 五劳七伤往后瞧 (Wǔ Láo Qī Shāng Wǎng Hòu Qiáo)

Meaning: "Looking Backward to Prevent the Five Strains and Seven Harms"

This deceptively simple movement is one of the most therapeutic exercises in the Baduanjin sequence. The slow, deliberate neck rotation releases tension accumulated from long hours at desks and screens, while the backward gaze is believed in TCM to stimulate the Du Mai (Governing Vessel) meridian that runs along the spine.

Step-by-Step Instructions

Step 1: Starting Position

Stand with feet shoulder-width apart. Place your hands at your sides with palms facing outward, fingers pointing down. Stand tall with your spine elongated and your chin slightly tucked.

Step 2: Turn the Head

Slowly turn your head to the left, looking as far behind you as comfortable. Keep your shoulders facing forward—only your head and neck rotate. At the same time, rotate your palms to face backward and open your chest.

Step 3: Gaze Behind

Hold the gaze for 3-5 seconds. Imagine an owl turning its head to look behind. Breathe deeply and feel the stretch along the right side of your neck and the opening across your chest.

Step 4: Return to Center

Slowly bring your head back to the center. Rotate your palms back to face your thighs. Relax for a moment before repeating on the right side.

Step 5: Alternate Sides

Repeat on the right side: turn your head to the right while opening your palms backward. Alternate sides for 6-8 repetitions total (3-4 per side).

Key Points

  • Move your head slowly and smoothly—never force the rotation
  • Keep your chin parallel to the ground; don't tilt it up or down
  • Your shoulders should stay level and relaxed throughout
  • The eyes should look as far back as comfortable, but don't strain
  • Breathe naturally; exhale as you turn, inhale as you return

Health Benefits

  • Relieves neck tension: Releases tightness from the sternocleidomastoid and trapezius muscles
  • Improves spinal mobility: Gentle rotation maintains and improves cervical spine flexibility
  • Reduces eye strain: The backward gaze exercises the eye muscles and improves focus
  • Stimulates the Governing Vessel: In TCM, this meridian governs the spine and brain
  • Prevents "five strains and seven harms": Traditional Chinese term for chronic fatigue and organ damage from overwork
"The owl sees all that others miss. By looking behind, we release what burdens us and make space for clarity." — Traditional Baduanjin instruction
